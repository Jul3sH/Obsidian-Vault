# Belief & Assumption Testing

> A core tool, not a side activity. Much of Julian's subjective, shifting decision-making rests on **untested beliefs** and **unclarified assumptions**. His gut runs on beliefs; his scenarios rest on assumptions. Surfacing and testing them is what turns a fuzzy, emotion-led decision into one he can trust enough to commit to.

This tool runs *inside* any framework ([[sharran-simple-decision-framework|Sharran's]], [[brain-brand-framework|BRAND]], [[seven-step-decision-process|7-step]]). It is the input-cleaning step.

## Why it matters for Julian specifically

- **The gut runs on beliefs.** "I'll be single in the UK," "a protected partner stops trying." Untested, they silently steer major decisions.
- **Scenarios rest on assumptions.** "I can't find well-paid work in HK." If the assumption is shaky, every scenario built on it is shaky, and the [[decision-maker-profile|scenario-churn]] never settles.
- **It operationalises the gut-vs-analysis rule.** The rule says the gut may override the analysis only with a written, justified reason. Belief-testing *is* that justification: a gut belief earns its veto only if it survives the test.

## The method

For the decision in front of you, list every load-bearing claim, then label each one:

| Label | What it is | What to do |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **Fact** | Verifiable, evidenced | Confirm the source, then trust it. |
| **Assumption** | Taken as true for now, but checkable | **Clarify it.** Seek evidence; where it's a probability, *quantify* it (e.g. P(well-paid HK work) = ?). |
| **Belief** | Subjective conviction, often emotional, usually untested | **Challenge it.** Is it true? What's the evidence? What would change my mind? Is this fear talking? |

Then **re-run the scenarios on the cleaned inputs.** Often the decision clarifies itself once a single shaky belief is downgraded or an assumption is quantified.

## Testing questions

**For an assumption:**
- What evidence supports this? What would I expect to see if it were false?
- Can I turn it into a probability or a number rather than a yes/no?
- Who would actually know, and have I asked them?

**For a belief (especially a fear-driven one):**
- Is this true, or is it a story I've told myself?
- What's the actual evidence, for and against?
- What would have to be true for the opposite to hold?
- If a friend said this, would I accept it, or push back?
- Is this fear wearing the costume of fact? (See [[commitment-avoidance|worst-case acceptance]].)

## Key Takeaways

- **Label before you analyse.** Fact / Assumption / Belief. Most "facts" steering a decision are actually untested beliefs.
- **Assumptions get clarified; beliefs get challenged.** Different moves.
- **Quantify where you can.** "I can't find work in HK" becomes "P(well-paid HK work) = X%." A probability can be reasoned with; a blanket belief can't.
- **This is how the gut earns its veto.** A belief that survives testing may legitimately override the analysis; one that doesn't, may not.
- **Clean inputs stop the churn.** Scenarios stop shifting your mind once the beliefs under them are settled.
