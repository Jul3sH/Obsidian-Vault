# Decision-Maker Profile - Julian

> A living model of how Julian actually makes decisions: his strengths, his failure mode, the emotional machinery underneath, and the design principles any framework must respect to work *for him*. This is the standing context that feeds every decision. Update it as evidence accumulates from the [[decision-journal|Decision Journal]].

**Why this exists:** Most decision advice is generic. It fails because it ignores the specific person running it. This profile makes the person explicit, so the [[choosing-a-decision-framework|framework picker]] can be tuned to Julian rather than to a textbook.

## The Core Pattern (one sentence)

**The analysis is never the weak link - the *commitment* is.** Julian produces rigorous, wide-framed analysis and then fails to convert it into a locked, acted-upon decision. He closes well only under deadline pressure; without a forcing function, he drifts.

> **v0.3 revision (7 Jul 2026, from the Fable meta-evaluation of the UK-move decision):** the weak link has *moved one step downstream*. Under this system he now **does** commit, on time (UK move committed 4 Jul, ahead of his own deadline; he acted within days). The new failure is **commitment durability**: *decides, then emotionally un-decides within ~72 hours.* The mechanism is specific - **his conviction re-anchors to the last emotionally warm voice, not the evidence** (every lean-change in the UK-move whipsaw timestamped to a human contact, never a document), and his absolute certainties ("100%") are emotional spikes that decay in days while his honest analysis speaks in ranges. The defence is the [[commitment-lock-protocol|Commitment-Lock Protocol]] (bias register, the F-N-M-T reopen test, the 48-hour stand-down, the wobble drill), enforced by the `commitment-guard` skill and the `feedback-decision-reopening` memory. **New hypotheses to watch (H1-H7)** are logged in the protocol doc: recency-of-contact dominance (H1, high), "100%-as-symptom" (H2), commit-by-proxy (H3), costume-rotation of the avoidance (H4), silent-stakeholders-lose (H5), the system-as-comfort-object (H6), and optimism in cash *timing* → the month-9 liquidity-feeling ambush (H7).

| Strength | Evidence |
|----------|----------|
| **Rigorous front-end analysis** | Hannah decision: widened options, opportunity-cost on both sides, financial impact analysis, scenario modelling, reality-tested the binary frame |
| **Considers fairness to others** | Analysed what was fair to *Hannah* as a separate branch, not just self-interest |

| **Excellent under pressure** | "I come alive" on high-stakes, deadline-driven complex deals - decisive when a deadline exists |

These are not the problem. Do not "fix" them. The system must *preserve* the analytical strength and bolt a commitment mechanism onto the end of it.

## The Commitment Gap (the central problem)

The failure mode, broken into its parts:

1. **Keeps options open by default** - ADHD + ENTP trait. Closing a door feels like loss. Recurs everywhere: relationships, the UK-move decision (live now), past deals, projects.
2. **Analysis as a safe harbour** - gathering more information *feels safer than concluding*. The motions of analysis substitute for the act of deciding.
3. **Never circles back** - does the research, builds the assessment, then doesn't return to *use* it to commit. Documents trail off ("Alternate option 2: TBA"). No scoring, no "therefore," no termination.
4. **Gut overrides analysis** - *the key mechanism.* When what the analysis points to differs from what the gut wants, he freezes - committing to either betrays the other. The gut holds veto power, so analysis can become theatre he never intended to obey, while he drifts to the gut by default.
5. **No deadline → no decision** - without external pressure the decision stays open until the world closes it for him (Hannah left for someone else; the decision got "made for him," badly).
6. **Scenario-churn (the flip-flop engine).** Runs scenarios endlessly in his head; each new scenario shifts the gut, so the decision never settles. There is always one more scenario. *Counter-move:* externalise scenarios onto paper as a fixed, frozen set, then stop generating new ones.

**Net effect:** not-deciding *is* a decision - the slowest, worst one. The cost is paid in lost options and regret.

## Decision Drivers (what he optimises for)

Ranked by observed weight, not stated preference. *To be refined.*

1. **Loss-aversion / financial security** - the master emotion. Don't get screwed, protect retirement, protect Sophia's future. Downstream of two events: the divorce (betrayal + financial loss) and cancer (mortality + provision anxiety).
2. **Optionality / freedom** - keeping paths open has intrinsic value to him (and intrinsic cost - see Commitment Gap).
3. **Fairness to others** - genuine weight on not harming the counterparty.
4. **Long-term happiness / compatibility** - present but frequently overridden by 1.

## Design Principles - what a framework must do FOR Julian

This is the actionable bridge. Any framework Julian adopts is judged against these:

1. **Impose an artificial deadline.** He closes only under pressure, so manufacture it. Every live decision gets a "decide by" date in the [[decision-journal|journal]].
2. **Force a termination step.** Analysis must end in an explicit, written "therefore I choose X." No "TBA." The framework is not complete until a door is closed.
3. **Surface gut vs. analysis explicitly, and make analysis the default winner.** Before committing, write down what the *analysis* says and what the *gut* wants, separately. **Rule (decided 2026-06-20): when they conflict, the analysis stands UNLESS the gut can justify the override with a named, written reason.** "I just feel it" is not enough. This stops emotional flip-flopping while still honouring a gut signal that carries real information.
4. **Make the decision irreversible-on-paper.** A committed decision is written, dated, and (where possible) announced or acted on, so reopening it has a cost.
5. **Don't reward option-proliferation.** Watch for the manufactured "third option" that exists only to defer the binary (e.g. the Bali coffee-shop escape hatch). Name it when it appears.
6. **Cheap, not academic.** Pragmatic and fast. Deep dissection is itself an ADHD avoidance behaviour. The process must feel "good enough to commit," not exhaustive.
7. **Test the beliefs and assumptions first.** Julian's gut runs on untested beliefs and his scenarios rest on unclarified assumptions. Before trusting either, surface the load-bearing claims and run [[belief-assumption-testing|Belief & Assumption Testing]]. This is also *how the gut earns its veto* under Principle 3: a belief may override the analysis only if it survives the test.
8. **Do groundwork first, and expect bounded iteration.** Real decisions are not linear (gather → choose → done). Establish the factual baseline before judging: current financials + runway, current relationship state, current snapshot of the destination, and the raw list of beliefs/assumptions. *Then* analyse. Expect to loop: analysis surfaces gaps and buried beliefs that need a little more research. Iteration is legitimate, but **gate it** - each loop must name the specific gap it closes and how it would change the decision; if it wouldn't change the decision, it is avoidance, skip it. The deadline holds regardless, and the good-enough bar is what ends the loop. ("I need a bit more research" is Julian's commitment-avoidance tell - the gate is the defence.)

## Commitment Protocol - locking a decision

Decided 2026-06-20. Every decision runs through these. At the start, ask: **"which devices apply to this decision?"**

**Universal (every decision):**

| Device | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **Deadline (decide-by date)** | Manufactures the pressure Julian needs to close. No deadline = no decision. |
| **Scheduled review date** | Makes locking safe: the decision is reversible-on-review, not forever. Removes the "but what if I'm wrong forever" brake. |
| **Good-enough bar** | A pre-agreed stopping point for analysis (see below). Prevents the endless "never enough information" loop. |

### Anchored deadlines (the deadline must be real)

Decided 2026-06-20. This is a root cause, not a detail. An arbitrary date has no teeth: Julian knows when a deadline is plucked from air, silently discards it, and leaves his options open. So a deadline only counts if it is *anchored*:

- **Natural anchor (preferred).** A real external forcing event with consequences: school enrolment cut-off, visa / residency timing, lease end, job-offer expiry, tax year, a counterparty's timeline. It has teeth on its own.
- **Manufactured anchor (only if no natural one exists).** Create teeth deliberately: a public commitment to a named person plus a real stake or consequence, openly acknowledged as self-imposed. A self-imposed date with no stake is not a deadline.

**The rule:** when you set a deadline, write the *evidence* for why it binds. No evidence, not a real deadline. The date need not be the final possible date, but the *choice* of date must be justified.

**The trap:** "researching the perfect deadline" or "deadline-setting as its own discipline" is itself deferral, the manufactured third option in disguise. The anchor almost always already exists in the real world. Find it, do not commission a study.

**Strongly recommended (use unless trivial):**

- **Pre-mortem with worst-case acceptance.** Write what would make this wrong, then apply Brian Tracy's fear-busting move: name the worst case and *accept* it. Julian is highly resilient (see Assets), so the worst case is almost always survivable. Naming and accepting it dissolves the fear that drives the indecision.

**Situational (decide per decision):**

- **Public commitment.** Tell someone. Adds a social cost to reopening. Right for big, identity-level decisions; overkill for small ones.

## Definition of Good Enough (not Definition of Done)

*Open work item.* "We never have enough information" is the engine of Julian's analysis paralysis, so "done" is the wrong test - it never arrives. The right test is **good enough**: the point at which more information would not change the decision enough to justify the delay. This needs a concrete, reusable definition (and possibly a per-decision-type version). **Next action: define this with Julian.**

## The Acceptance Test for Commitment

Julian commits when he *feels the process was good enough that the result is as good as he'll get.* This is the real lever. The job of a framework is not to find the optimal answer, it is to **earn enough trust in the process that the gut stops fighting it.** Design for "trustworthy enough to lock," not "provably optimal."

## Assets to Deploy (counterweights)

Positive traits to actively lean on as antidotes to the failure mode:

- **Resilience / survivor identity.** Julian's own words: *"I am a survivor. I make things work. It might be uncomfortable, but it works out in the end."* This is the direct counterweight to loss-aversion. Deploy it deliberately in the pre-mortem: the worst case is survivable, therefore the irreversible-mistake fear is overweighted. He has asked for *more* "it'll work out" and *less* risk-aversion thinking.
- **Genuine analytical rigour.** Already strong; the system preserves it rather than adding to it.
