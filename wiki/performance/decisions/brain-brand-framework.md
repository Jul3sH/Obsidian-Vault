# BRAIN / BRAND Framework

A structured checklist for evaluating a decision before committing. Originally developed for medical informed consent but applies to any decision where you have a clear option in mind and need to pressure-test it before acting.

Three variants exist - choose based on whether you want intuition included and whether you need a formal decision prompt at the end.

## The Variants

| Letter | BRAIN | BRAN | BRAND | BRAINED |
|--------|-------|------|-------|---------|
| **B** | Benefits | Benefits | Benefits | Benefits |
| **R** | Risks | Risks | Risks | Risks |
| **A** | Alternatives | Alternatives | Alternatives | Alternatives |
| **I** | Intuition (gut feeling) | - | - | Intuition |
| **N** | Need time / Nothing (is inaction better?) | Need time / Nothing | Need time / Nothing | Need time / Nothing |
| **D** | - | - | Decision (explicit final choice) | Decision |

**On the name:** the elements spell **B-R-A-I-N-D** (six elements). "BRAINED" is the readable spelling only - the E is not a step. BRAINED is simply BRAIN **and** the D step together: every element kept, none dropped.

### Which variant to use

| Variant | Use when |
|---------|----------|
| **BRAIN** | You want to include gut feeling as a legitimate input |
| **BRAN** | You want to stay objective and remove personal bias |
| **BRAND** | You want a formal close - the D forces an explicit commitment at the end |
| **BRAINED** | You want *both* the gut named (I) and a forced final choice (D) - all six elements |

**Default recommendation:** BRAND. The D step prevents the framework from ending in ambiguity.

**Julian's preferred variant: BRAINED.** It keeps every element - the **I** step names the gut as a legitimate input (his gut is often load-bearing) and the **D** step forces a dated commitment (his diagnosed failure mode is commitment, not analysis - see [[decision-maker-profile]]). Framing cons as **R**isks also lets fear-driven beliefs be probability-tested via [[belief-assumption-testing]] rather than accepted as fact. First applied to the UK relocation decision - see [[why-brained]].

## The Questions to Ask

- **Benefits:** What are the benefits of this option?
- **Risks:** What are the downsides and potential harms?
- **Alternatives:** What other options exist?
- **Need time / Nothing:** Do I need more time? Is doing nothing a better option?
- **Decision:** Based on the above, what am I choosing?

## When to Use

- You have a clear decision in front of you and need to evaluate it before committing
- Binary or limited-option decisions (yes/no, option A vs option B)
- Medium stakes - high enough to warrant structured evaluation, low enough that full 7-step research isn't needed
- Time available: 15-60 minutes

## How It Compares to the 7-Step Process

| 7-Step | BRAND coverage |
|--------|----------------|
| 1. Identify the decision | Assumed - you already know what you're deciding |
| 2. Gather information | Assumed - you have enough to evaluate |
| 3. Identify alternatives | Yes (A) |
| 4. Weigh the evidence | Partial - B and R cover pros/cons but not a full matrix |
| 5. Choose among alternatives | Yes (D in BRAND) |
| 6. Take action | Not covered |
| 7. Review | Not covered |

BRAND is not a full process - it is a pre-commitment evaluation tool. Pair it with [[sharran-simple-decision-framework|Sharran's Step 4]] (map next steps) once the D step is done.

## Key Takeaways

- The N step (Need time / Nothing) is the most important for avoiding sunk-cost decisions - it gives you explicit permission to pause or walk away.
- BRAND is not a replacement for problem definition. If the problem is still fuzzy, run [[sharran-simple-decision-framework|Sharran's Step 2]] (Isolate the issue) first.
- The I step in BRAIN is valid - intuition encodes pattern-matched experience. Don't discard it; just name it explicitly so it can be weighed alongside evidence.
