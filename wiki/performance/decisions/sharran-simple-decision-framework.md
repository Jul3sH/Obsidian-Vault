# Sharran's Simple Decision Framework

A four-step framework for structured decision-making from entrepreneur and CEO Sharran Srivatsaa. Built on the premise that most poor decisions happen because there is no framework at all - not because the person lacks intelligence.

## The Framework

| Step | Label | The question to ask |
|------|-------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Understand the context** | Where is the shared information? What do we know? |
| 2 | **Isolate the issue** | What are we actually solving? |
| 3 | **Accept the risk** | If we make this decision, what actually breaks? |
| 4 | **Map the decision** | What are the next steps? |

## In Sharran's Words

> *"Most people don't have a framework for making decisions. Therefore, they make dumb decisions. From that day, I started thinking about how I make decisions, and then I made a framework for how to make decisions:*
>
> *1. Understand the context. You ask where the shared information is.*
> *2. You isolate the issue. Hey, what are we actually solving?*
> *3. You accept the risk. If we make this decision, what actually breaks?*
> *4. You map the decision. Hey, what am I going to do as next steps?*
>
> *Now, when you have a framework for making decisions and someone brings a problem to you, you're like, 'Wait, can you tell me about that? Understand the facts.' Then you say, 'So the problem we're trying to solve is this?' Isolate issues. Number three, accept the risk. If we make this decision, we probably can do this project, this project, this project, and this project. Yes, accept the risk. Number four, okay, even though we're going to do all that, what is the next step? You make a decision, right?"*

## Key Takeaways

- A framework beats intelligence: having any structured process produces better decisions than raw instinct, regardless of how smart the decision-maker is.
- Step 2 (Isolate the issue) is the most commonly conflated - people solve the symptom, not the problem, because they skip defining exactly what they are solving.
- Step 3 (Accept the risk) is the most commonly avoided - naming what breaks feels like commitment, so people skip it and remain vague. Naming it is the point.
- The framework runs in real-time: all four steps can be completed in a 5-minute conversation. It is not a process for committees; it is a mental discipline for a single decision-maker.
- "Accept the risk" does not mean ignore or minimise it - it means explicitly acknowledge what breaks, then decide anyway with eyes open.
