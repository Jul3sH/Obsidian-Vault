# Visual Representation Bias in Decision-Making

> *When a vivid but incomplete mental image substitutes for analysis - and how to complete it.*

## The Pattern

When evaluating a decision option, Julian forms a strong visual representation of what life under that option would look like. This image tends to:

- **Crystallise around the most appealing moments** - picking Sophia up from school, a comfortable room, an easy morning
- **Omit the parts that define lived experience** - the early commute, the crash-pad nights, the absent social life, the financial vigilance
- **Feel like intuition** when it is an incomplete picture, not a considered view

The image builds fast and builds strong. Once vivid, it functions as evidence: the option *feels* good, and that feeling is mistaken for a considered view.

## Reference Case: Malvern Permanent (15 Jul 2026)

Julian was evaluating Variant B of [[malvern-permanent-feasibility-2026-07-14|permanent-Malvern]] (base at Mum's, London commute 3 days/week for 6 years). The image in his head: sitting in Mum's front room, picking Sophia up from school. That image warmed him to the option. The image he needed - getting up early three times a week, commuting 2.5 hours each way, staying somewhere crappy on Tuesday night, no social life, no girlfriend, rootless for six years - was absent.

Once both images were surfaced and compared, the option failed on its own terms. His own words: *"it's the worst of everything."* The numbers already showed it was dominated. But the incomplete image was pulling him toward it anyway - which is why surfacing it explicitly mattered.

Logged at Julian's request: *"I have to be very careful of it because I started to warm to the idea of being long-term with my Mum's... The vision of getting up early, commuting, staying in a shit place, not having a girlfriend - I needed to focus on that one."*

## Why This Happens (likely ADHD-amplified)

- **Vivid prospective imagery.** Mental representations of the future are emotionally compelling and resist abstract counter-argument. "The numbers show it's dominated" is less powerful than "the image says it's pleasant."
- **Emotional valence builds fast.** The pleasant frame crystallises first and anchors the evaluation; the unpleasant frame rarely builds spontaneously.
- **Fatigue amplifies it.** When tired, the brain pulls toward whichever option feels easiest. The image of ease wins against a careful analysis of cost.

See [[adhd-aware-work-patterns]] for the broader ADHD pattern set.

## The Intervention: Complete the Image

When evaluating an option with emotional pull, ask: **"What does the ordinary bad day under this option look like?"** Not the worst case. Not a disaster scenario. Just a regular Tuesday when things are fine but not great.

If the bad-day image is materially worse than was pictured: name it, log it in the subject's own words, and ask whether it changes the weighting. If both images are present and the option still wins: accept it. That is a genuine preference, not a bias-driven one.

This is embedded in the [[decision-visualisation-check]] skill: the check fires automatically when Julian is evaluating life, career, or relationship options and expresses a preference based on feel.

## Key Takeaways

- Strong visual representations are not evidence - they are emotional thumbnails that need to be completed, not trusted at face value
- The pleasant image builds first; the unpleasant image must be deliberately constructed
- Fatigue and time pressure make the bias worse
- The intervention is simple: ask for both images, not just the one that formed naturally
- This check fires BEFORE commitment; [[commitment-guard]] defends the decision AFTER commitment
