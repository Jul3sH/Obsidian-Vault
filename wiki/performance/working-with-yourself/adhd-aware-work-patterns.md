# ADHD-Aware Work Patterns

## Know the Traits

Understanding your own ADHD tendencies is a competitive advantage if you design your work around them:

- **Hyper-focus:** Once genuinely started on the right thing, execution is fast and deep
- **Research loops:** Curiosity without constraints disappears into rabbit holes for hours/days
- **Distraction pulls:** Strong urge to pick up the phone, switch tasks, start something new after brief exertion
- **Analysis paralysis:** Endless deliberation without a forcing function leads to doing nothing
- **Bottom-up drift:** Without urgency, defaults to planning, studying, and organising instead of delivering
- **Deadline activation:** The real focus often only arrives under genuine time pressure

## Strategies That Work

### Pomodoro / Timed Focus Blocks
- Work in contained sprints; allow the distraction urge to exist but defer it
- "I can have that distraction every 30 minutes" is enough to resist the pull in the moment
- Phone out of sight — the urge fades within a minute if resisted

### Schedule Work With Others
- Accountability and social commitment override the internal drag toward distraction
- When work is scheduled with others (team members, meetings, co-working), focus shifts immediately to doing
- Commit to doing things with people to force your own getting-started moment
- This is the antidote to analysis paralysis: once you start with someone else, hyper-focus takes over

### Timebox Learning Ruthlessly
- Unscoped learning is the highest-risk activity for ADHD drift
- Always set a maximum time allocation before beginning any research or learning
- Create a scoped list of what you need to know; tick through it; stop when done
- CCIE example: years of drifting vs. months of success once a structured timetable was enforced

### Minimal Investigation Before Starting
- Do minimal research, then start the deliverable — research actual roadblocks as they appear
- Mental barriers built through over-research are usually false
- The roadblocks you anticipated are rarely the real ones

### Work to Objectively Prioritised Schedules
- ADHD prioritisation drifts toward the interesting, the comfortable, and the familiar
- Counter this by building an objective priority list based on cost/impact (not interest)
- Review it daily; treat deviations as violations, not flexibility
- Having a pre-committed schedule removes the decision from the moment — you just follow it

### Build vs Adopt — a System Isn't Done When It's Designed, It's Done When It's Used
- The ADHD reward is in *designing* a system (novel, creative, open-ended), not *running* it (repetitive, boring, executive-function-heavy). Systems get built and silently abandoned the moment they stop being novel
- This is distinct from dropping a *working* system for a shiny one — here the system is never adopted in the first place. It looks like productivity (you built something), but it produced nothing because it was never used
- A new system is not "finished" at design. It's finished when it has been *used* for a set period (e.g. two weeks) and survived the boredom
- Every new system needs an **adoption forcing-function** baked in at creation, not just a design: a scheduled first-use, a trigger that starts it, or an accountability surface that flags non-use. Without one, the build is wasted effort
- Track every system you build and its live/lapsed status in the [[systems-register]] — an "Abandoned" label next to days of build effort is the accountability signal
- Related failure at the starting gate: the [[self-sabotage-and-fear|false prerequisite]] — talking yourself out of ever *using* it by inventing a setup requirement

### Manage Distractions Actively
- Know your scheduled priorities before each day begins — mental note, not just a diary glance
- When a distraction appears, compare it explicitly to your schedule: "Is this more important than X?"
- Recognise that many "convincing distractions" are fear-driven avoidance of the real priority — including the *false prerequisite* ("I can't start until I've built the perfect X"); see [[self-sabotage-and-fear]]

## Context Switching — The Hyperfocus Problem

Context switching is disproportionately costly with ADHD. Once hyperfocus is active, being forced to switch tasks doesn't feel like an inconvenience — it feels like a threat.

**Why it's harder:**
- **Hyperfocus inertia:** The ADHD brain in hyperfocus is "all-on" — disengaging feels like pulling a leg from quicksand
- **Executive function:** Starting, stopping, and switching tasks requires more deliberate effort than it does for others
- **Working memory:** Holding two task-sets in mind while transitioning is harder, leading to errors and frustration
- **Emotional reactivity:** Interruptions during hyperfocus trigger intense irritation, often disproportionate to the interruption — this is a neurological response, not a character flaw

**The specific pattern:** Initial resistance to a task → forced engagement → hyperfocus locks in → intense irritation at any new intrusion, even tasks you were previously resistant to. The task you resisted yesterday and now own completely is the one you'll most aggressively defend from interruption.

**Strategies:**
- Give yourself a defined window before switching — even 10 minutes of "let me reach a stopping point"
- When interrupted, write down exactly where you are before switching — returning to a written thread is easier than reconstructing from memory
- Build context switches into your schedule so they are expected, not surprise intrusions
- Communicate to others that mid-hyperfocus interruptions have a cost; build this into how you manage expectations

## The Urgency Trick

Since focus often only arrives under deadline pressure, manufacture the urgency:
- Book the exam; commit the deadline publicly; schedule a delivery meeting
- Use the Pomodoro timer to create artificial sprints
- Think: "What would I do if I had 2 hours to produce this?" — then do exactly that

## My Pattern

This is one of the most directly applicable articles in the wiki — ADHD traits are the operating system underneath most of the performance challenges documented here. The hyper-focus reward is real: once genuinely started, execution is fast and deep. The problem is always getting started, and the drift toward research, planning, and organising instead of delivering. The distraction pull — phone, new idea, switching tasks after brief exertion — is a constant background force that requires active systems (Pomodoro, scheduling with others) to contain, not willpower alone.

## Key Takeaways

- Hyper-focus is the reward for getting started — the hard part is the start, not the doing
- Schedule work with others to break the starting inertia; your focus will follow
- Timebox all learning: without constraints, curiosity is the most expensive distraction
- Manufacture urgency and constraints — they're what activate the best performance mode
- Pomodoro or sprint-based work respects the ADHD attention cycle rather than fighting it
- Pre-committed schedules remove in-moment decisions and make discipline easier to maintain
