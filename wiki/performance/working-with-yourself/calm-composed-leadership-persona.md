# Calm, Composed Leadership Persona

## The Persona to Cultivate

At a senior level, people are not looking for enthusiasm or energy — they are looking for quiet, composed reassurance that comes with experience.

- Puppy-dog enthusiasm signals youth and inexperience
- The wry, measured smile beats the big grin every time
- Calm authority in meetings reads as wisdom; excitability reads as inexperience

## Behave Like the Leader You Want to Be

- Leadership behaviour is often contrived at first — that is fine, do it anyway
- Model the leaders you want to emulate; practice at every opportunity
- At your level of seniority, there is an expectation of how leaders behave — meet it, even if it feels stiff

Specific behaviours to lock in:
- **Never huff and puff** about a tough task or request — it signals you can't handle it
- **Never lose control in public** — including social situations and after alcohol
- **React professionally to tough requests** — receive them calmly, ask clarifying questions, respond with a plan
- **Never go over-familiar** — impressions, laddish banter, and wisecracks undermine authority and give people ammunition
- **Resist the urge to crack jokes** in unfamiliar rooms — if rapport isn't established, a risky joke always lands badly

## Assertiveness and Confidence

- Life is too short to be a wimp — assert your opinion, sell it, push back on decisions you disagree with
- Be aggressive (in the positive sense): if you don't sell your view, the decision will go another way
- Put the fear of god into them that they will get it wrong if they don't listen
- Assertiveness is not aggression — it's confident, data-backed conviction

## Defensiveness — the Silent Credibility Killer

- When challenged or questioned, the instinct is to over-explain quickly — this reads as defensive and insecure
- Experts speak slowly, calmly, and with quiet authority — they don't need to justify themselves urgently
- Technique: slow it down, pause, respond in a calm and authoritative way
- Dispelling a perception works better through composed confidence than through an anxious flood of explanation

## Alcohol and Social Situations

- Losing control socially (getting drunk, being preachy, oversharing) undoes the professional persona you've built
- If people see you lose control outside work, they question your control inside it
- The rule: create an impression, then know when to step back — don't over-deliver

## The Cool Persona

- Dedicate time to cultivating the image: the reassured, measured professional
- In video calls and meetings, watch yourself — big expressive reactions look amateurish
- Wry, controlled, thoughtful beats animated and eager

## My Pattern

The over-familiar tendency and defensive over-explaining are recurring patterns requiring deliberate practice to correct. Social situations present the highest risk for the persona slipping. Slowing down and responding from calm authority requires consistent discipline.

## Key Takeaways

- At senior level, quiet composure signals experience and credibility — enthusiasm signals the opposite
- Behave like the leader you want to be, even before it comes naturally
- Never huff, puff, crack jokes in untested rooms, or go over-familiar — all of these give people ammunition
- Defensiveness is always perceived as insecurity — slow down, pause, and respond from calm authority
- Public loss of control (social or professional) damages the persona permanently — protect it at all times
