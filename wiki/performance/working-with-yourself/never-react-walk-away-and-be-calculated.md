# Never React — Walk Away and Be Calculated

## The Pattern

The same lesson emerged across hundreds of situations: reacting in the heat of the moment consistently made things worse. Whether a colleague was being insubordinate, a message felt like an attack, or a mistake had just been made — the instinctive reactive response never helped.

## Why Reacting Fails

- It gives the other person ammunition and shifts the narrative away from their behaviour
- It lowers you to the other person's level, especially when you hold seniority
- It signals loss of control, which damages credibility and authority
- It escalates situations that would otherwise resolve themselves
- Assumptions made in the moment are frequently wrong (the Facebook message incident; the email that "proved" you wrong)

## The Framework: Calm, Collected, Calculated

**Step 1 — Walk away**
- Physical distance creates mental space
- Go to the gym, sleep on it, take a walk before responding
- Ask yourself: "Has anything actually happened here yet?"

**Step 2 — Get the facts**
- Never respond aggressively to something you haven't verified
- Run scenarios where you could be wrong before acting on the assumption you're right
- Draft responses and leave them for an hour before sending

**Step 3 — Respond with precision**
- Be firm but professional — you can hold your ground without being reactive
- Paraphrase the other person's position before countering (shows you listened, not just reacted)
- Solution-oriented language only — no venting, no whingeing

## Specific Situations

- **Conflict with a subordinate:** Don't react to insubordination directly; reframe and appeal to their interests
- **Feeling accused or shown up:** Check you have the facts 100% right before querying; there's no rush to defend yourself
- **Making a big mistake:** Smile, minimise internally ("no one has died"), then create a plan to fix it calmly
- **Hostile email:** Leave the draft for an hour, double-check facts, then respond at a measured tone
- **Conflict in public:** Remove yourself from the setting before responding — public arguments are always losses

## Reframe

Reacting is not strength — it is giving someone else control of your behaviour. The calculated response is the powerful one.

## My Pattern

The pattern is consistent: emotionally-charged information is processed as certainty, an aggressive response is composed, and only later (or through deliberate pause) is it discovered that the assumption was incorrect. The lesson — "Has anything actually happened here yet?" — is a reliable reset question for exactly this scenario.

## Key Takeaways

- Never send, say, or do anything in the heat of the moment — create a mandatory cool-off period
- Get the facts before responding; assumptions made under emotion are usually wrong
- "Does nothing actually happened yet? Then chill" is a reliable reset question
- Firm and professional is not the same as reactive — you can hold your ground without losing control
- Reacting lowers you to others' level and hands them the power; the calculated pause is always the stronger play
