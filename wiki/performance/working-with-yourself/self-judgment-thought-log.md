# Self-Judgment Thought Log

> A running log for thoughts that damage judgment, confidence, or action. Capture the thought, name the distortion, extract the lesson, and turn it into a behaviour rule.

## Key Takeaways

- A feeling of rejection is not evidence of rejection.
- Silence from other people is weak evidence: they may not know what is happening, may be busy, or may assume no help is needed.
- Do not let untested assumptions about reputation become self-punishment.
- The corrective move is outreach, not rumination.
- Repeated thoughts should compound into explicit behaviour rules.

## How To Use This Log

| Field | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Date | When the thought was captured |
| Trigger | What set it off |
| Automatic thought | The raw thought, without polishing it |
| Distortion / risk | The thinking error or behavioural risk |
| Corrective interpretation | The more accurate read |
| Behaviour rule | What to do next time |
| Linked lesson | Where the pattern should compound |

## Pattern Map

| Pattern | Why it matters | Counter-move |
|---|---|---|
| Mind-reading silence | "They have not contacted me" gets misread as "they do not rate me" | Treat silence as no data until tested |
| Rejection sensitivity | Possible ADHD-linked rejection sensitivity turns ambiguity into pain | Name the feeling, then ask what evidence actually exists |
| Reputation anxiety | Fear of being judged blocks useful outreach | Ask for help or advice before deciding what others think |
| Avoidance disguised as realism | "Maybe I should not contact them" feels sensible, but protects against possible rejection | Send a small, low-pressure message |

## Behaviour Rules To Compound

- When a thought says "they would have contacted me if I mattered", translate it to: "they may not know I need anything."
- When reputation anxiety appears, take one evidence-gathering action before accepting the story.
- Contact first, interpret second.
- Keep outreach low-pressure: ask for perspective, advice, leads, or a quick catch-up, not rescue.
- Judge the response, not the imagined response.
