# Commitment Avoidance (The Open-Options Trap)

> A recurring, named behavioural pattern: Julian keeps options open past the point of usefulness, substitutes analysis for commitment, and lets decisions be made by default. Documented here because it shows up far beyond any single decision - in relationships, the live UK-move question, past deals, and projects - and addressing it is a primary goal of the AI OS.

This is the *behavioural* article. The *decision-method* response to it lives in [[decision-maker-profile|Decision-Maker Profile]] and [[choosing-a-decision-framework|the framework picker]].

## What it looks like

- Keeping every door open feels like freedom; closing one feels like loss.
- Doing more research / analysis *feels safer than concluding* - so the analysis expands and the decision never lands.
- Going through the motions of a process without circling back to *use* it (the research gets done, the choice doesn't).
- Manufacturing a "third option" whose real function is to **defer** a binary decision.
- Performing well only when an external deadline forces closure; drifting badly when there is none.

## Why it happens (working hypothesis)

| Driver | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| **ADHD** | Novelty-seeking + aversion to closure; urgency only switches on under pressure. "I come alive" under a deadline, stall without one. |
| **ENTP wiring** | Genuine preference for open-ended optionality; closing options feels like a loss of possibility. |
| **Loss-aversion** | Committing risks the irreversible wrong-choice; staying open avoids the felt loss (while incurring a larger hidden one). |
| **Gut-vs-analysis conflict** | When the gut and the analysis disagree, freezing avoids betraying either. See [[decision-maker-profile|profile]]. |

## Why it's costly

Not-deciding is itself a decision - usually the slowest and worst one. The clearest case: a four-year relationship where the analysis was done and the gut had effectively chosen, but no commitment was made and no deadline existed. The decision got made externally, badly. The open option didn't preserve value; it destroyed it.

## Counter-moves (the fixes)

These are the same forcing functions used in [[adhd-aware-work-patterns|ADHD-Aware Work Patterns]] and [[prioritisation-and-scheduling|Prioritisation & Scheduling]], applied to decisions:

1. **Manufacture a deadline.** No deadline = no decision, so invent one and treat it as real. Every live decision gets a "decide-by" date.
2. **Force a termination step.** A process isn't done until a door is closed in writing: "Therefore I choose X." Ban "TBA."
3. **Name the escape hatch.** When a convenient "third option" appears, ask: is this a real path, or a way to avoid choosing? Often the latter.
4. **Separate gut from analysis, then reconcile on purpose.** Write both down; resolve the conflict deliberately instead of drifting to the gut by default.
5. **Make commitment costly to reverse.** Write it, date it, say it out loud, act on the first step - so reopening has friction.
6. **Lower the bar from "optimal" to "good enough to commit."** Perfection-seeking is avoidance in disguise (see [[perfectionism-vs-speed-of-delivery|Perfectionism vs Speed of Delivery]] and [[beating-procrastination-face-fear-get-started|Beating Procrastination]]).
7. **Bust the fear: name the worst case and accept it.** Much of the avoidance is fear-driven. Brian Tracy's fear-busting move - state the worst realistic outcome, then accept it - shrinks the fear to size. Julian is highly resilient ("I am a survivor, I make things work, it works out in the end"), so the worst case is almost always survivable. Deploy this identity deliberately as the antidote to loss-aversion. See [[decision-maker-profile|profile]] Assets to Deploy.

## The U-turn variant (added 7 Jul 2026)

The pattern has a second act, discovered in the UK-move decision. Once a forcing function *does* get Julian to commit, the avoidance doesn't vanish - it **rotates costume** and reappears as an **emotional U-turn** ~72 hours later. He reopens a settled decision, re-anchoring to the last warm human contact rather than any new evidence.

- **Costume rotation:** the *stated* reason for reopening changes each time (singlehood → "test the relationship" → "protect the bottom line") but the *structure* is constant - defer the full commitment. Naming the likely next costumes in advance (in the decision's anchor doc) converts ambush into forecast.
- **The tell:** a spike to certainty ("100% convinced") right after an emotionally warm conversation. Treat >~90% conviction as a warning light, not a green light.
- **The fix is not more analysis (that feeds it) but a *defence of the close*:** the [[commitment-lock-protocol|Commitment-Lock Protocol]] - the F-N-M-T reopen test, a 48-hour stand-down after any warm contact, and a 60-second wobble drill. A wobble is information about who you spoke to, not about the decision.

## Key Takeaways

- The weak link is **commitment, not analysis.** Don't build more analysis; build forcing functions.
- Commitment now happens - but **staying committed** is the new skill. Guard the close with the [[commitment-lock-protocol|Commitment-Lock Protocol]]; a post-decision wobble is forecast, not evidence.
- **Deadlines are the master fix** - Julian closes well under pressure, so the system manufactures pressure.
- Watch for the **manufactured third option**; it is usually deferral wearing the costume of strategy.
- Track instances in the [[decision-journal|Decision Journal]] so the pattern stays visible and the fixes get tested.
