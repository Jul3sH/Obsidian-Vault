# Perfectionism vs Speed of Delivery

## The Perfectionism Trap

Perfectionism manifests as:
- Over-committing (promising too much to sound impressive, then scrambling to deliver)
- Over-engineering (spending time on elaborate systems when quick-and-done would suffice)
- Dropping working habits in favour of shiny new systems that haven't been tested
- Refusing to ship until something is ideal — which means never shipping

The tell: you're spending more time organising, planning, and optimising than actually delivering.

## Over-Commitment

- Saying yes to everything to sound capable creates a backlog that produces stress and missed deadlines
- Over-commitment at work is one of the biggest personal stress drivers
- The rule: only commit to what you can deliver, and then deliver it without exception
- Under-promise, over-deliver builds a reputation — over-promise and scramble destroys it
- Set realistic expectations from the start; adjust them proactively, not reactively

## Quick Wins Beat Perfectionism

- Delivering something small and complete breaks the perfectionism loop
- A working rough draft creates momentum; an ideal draft in your head creates nothing
- Quick wins are visible, build confidence, and demonstrate progress to others
- Perfectionism on a communication mind-map vs. just getting cards into Anki: the quick win wins every time

## Don't Drop Good Habits

- When something is working, don't abandon it for something new and shiny
- Old robust systems that you've optimised are more valuable than fresh untested ones
- If you've put work in to make a process good, the bar for replacing it must be high
- Review and confirm that new approaches are actually improvements before switching

## Test Systems in Production — Small Changes

- Never radically overhaul a working system in one go
- Make one small change, test it in real conditions, assess the result, then make the next change
- Radical theoretical overhauls (based on what looks good in a different context) create chaos
- Example: revamped a working email system for a different environment — 6 months of chaos before reverting to something close to the original

## Be Honest About What's Working

- Don't persevere for months with a broken system out of sunk cost or stubbornness
- Create, get started, then regularly ask: "Is this working as I intended?"
- Spot the signal that something isn't working early — change it cleanly rather than grinding against it

## Commitments and Delivery

- Committing to deliver something and then not delivering is reputation-damaging, even when nobody makes a fuss
- It signals to people (and yourself) that you don't follow through
- Every on-time delivery builds the muscle; every late delivery erodes it
- Practice delivering on time even for low-stakes items — it builds the habit for high-stakes ones

## My Pattern

Over-commitment is a recurring stress driver — saying yes to demonstrate capability, then carrying the anxiety of a backlog that can't realistically be cleared. Perfectionism in learning shows up as spending time designing a perfect system rather than doing the imperfect thing that actually produces results.

## Key Takeaways

- Perfectionism is a form of procrastination — deliver something imperfect and iterate
- Only commit to what you can deliver; then deliver it on time, every time
- Quick wins build confidence and momentum — waiting for perfect builds nothing
- Don't drop good systems for shiny new ones without testing in production first
- Make small changes and test them; never overhaul a working system in one go
- Regularly ask "is this working?" and change course early rather than grinding against a broken system
