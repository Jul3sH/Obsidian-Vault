# Prioritisation and Scheduling

## The Fundamental Problem with Lists

- Long to-do lists create the illusion of productivity while enabling endless reshuffling
- If you have 6 tasks and consistently achieve 1-2, you've built a system that lets you off the hook
- Clue: if you're reviewing the same list every morning and weekend without progress, the system is broken

## The Fix: Commit, Schedule, Monitor

**Commit** — not "I intend to do this" but "my life depends on it"
- Making a strong internal commitment (like booking an exam) forces real prioritisation
- Without commitment, more interesting distractions always win

**Schedule** — block actual time
- Strict but realistic schedules force you to understand the impact of slippage
- Without a deadline, scope expands indefinitely (Parkinson's Law)
- Timebox everything: learning especially, because curiosity creates unlimited rabbit holes

**Monitor** — know where you are daily
- Each morning, mentally take note of scheduled priorities — not just a glance at the diary
- Know every deadline so you can be assertive about managing distractions

## Critical Path Thinking

- Identify the highest-consequence, irreversible activities first
- Complete critical path items before everything else — always
- Ask: "What is the one thing that, if I get it wrong, cannot be undone?"
- Example: spending time building slides before checking whether the technical approach was even viable wasted days

## Prioritisation Must Be Objective, Not Subjective

- Subjective prioritisation means drifting toward work you find interesting or comfortable — usually the wrong work
- Tie priority to tangible cost/impact: "If I don't do X by Y, it costs me £Z or opportunity A"
- Fear disguises itself as prioritisation — you'll deprioritise the scary, important thing and convince yourself something else was more urgent

**Fear-based deprioritisation patterns to watch for:**
- Studying technical content instead of applying for jobs
- Over-preparing for one path while ignoring a higher-value option
- Labelling procrastination as "I'm not ready yet"

## Agile Prioritisation

- When priorities change or new information emerges, adapt immediately
- Being locked into a plan that no longer makes sense (because you committed to it) is not discipline, it's rigidity
- Ask constantly: "Is this still the best use of my time, given what I know now?"

## Email and Inbox Management

- The inbox is a distraction machine — treat it aggressively
- Rule: focus only on deliverables you're responsible for, plus urgent requests from key stakeholders
- Search by name or topic when you need something — don't process reactively

## Convincing Distractions

- The most dangerous distractions look like legitimate work
- Learning feels productive but is often avoidance of the real priority
- Challenge yourself: "Is this linked to my primary objective right now, or is it a cloaked distraction?"

## Timebox Learning

- Learning without a scoped timetable and deadline disappears into rabbit holes
- Create a comprehensive list of what needs to be learned, allocate time, and enforce it
- The CCIE example: years of drifting vs. months of focused scheduled study once a strict plan was in place

## My Pattern

Fear-based deprioritisation is the most costly pattern: studying interesting content instead of pursuing higher-value options, labelling procrastination as "not ready yet." Subjective interest consistently wins out over objective importance without forced discipline and real commitment.

## Key Takeaways

- Commit + Schedule + Monitor beats any to-do list system
- Prioritise based on tangible cost/impact, not interest or comfort
- Identify critical path activities daily and protect them from all distractions
- Fear disguises itself as legitimate reprioritisation — name it and override it
- Agile prioritisation: when information changes, reprioritise without sentiment
- Timebox learning — curiosity without constraints consumes everything
