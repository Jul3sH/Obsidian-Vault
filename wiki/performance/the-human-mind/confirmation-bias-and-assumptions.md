# Confirmation Bias and Assumptions

## What It Is
- **Confirmation bias** is the tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms existing beliefs — while ignoring or discounting contradictory evidence
- It shows up in troubleshooting, decision-making, feedback, learning, and everyday conversations
- It feels like certainty; it is actually selective filtering

## How It Manifests

### Jumping to conclusions without due diligence
- Overwhelming supporting evidence can still lead to wrong conclusions — coincidences are common, especially in complex systems
- Always investigate causes fully; never let "it must be X" substitute for actually verifying X
- Ask constantly: *"Do I know this as a fact, or am I making an assumption?"*

### Communicating before you have the facts
- Stating definitive positions before confirming them damages credibility
- When uncertain, qualify: *"Based on the information currently available…"*
- Don't create a fuss until an event actually happens — wait until the critical point, then act if still necessary

### Ignoring inconvenient evidence
- Skipping uncomfortable conversations (e.g. not asking an accountant a question you fear the answer to) leads to avoidable mistakes
- Dismissing alerts or inconsistencies because they are inconvenient is a form of bias — check configs, check everything
- If evidence seems irrelevant, that is not a reason to ignore it — it is a reason to examine it more carefully

### Defending a position instead of evaluating options equally
- When you are convinced you are right, you stop applying creative thinking to alternative options
- The fix: consider each option with the same level of intellectual effort you apply to defending your own
- Actually listen to other people's opinions — if they differ from yours, treat that as data, not a threat

### Confirmation bias in feedback and learning
- When receiving feedback, the brain hunts for reasons to dismiss it — especially when already under pressure or failing
- Reading documentation or learning something new: notice when you are inventing elaborate theories to explain away contradictions
- Simple explanations are usually correct; resist the pull of interesting-sounding complexity

## Antidotes
- Pause and ask: *"Could I be going off on the wrong tangent? Is there a simpler explanation?"*
- Seek disconfirming evidence deliberately — actively look for reasons you might be wrong
- When something important is at stake, discuss it with the relevant expert even if you fear the answer
- Read/listen first, form conclusions later — not the other way around
- Spot the bias in real time: *"I have a story I want to be true here. Am I filtering evidence to fit it?"*

## My Pattern

Assuming certainty before checking facts is the core pattern. Stating definitive positions before fully verifying, then having to walk them back. Avoiding uncomfortable questions is a choice with consequences.

## Key Takeaways
- Confirmation bias is invisible from the inside — it feels like clarity, not distortion
- Overwhelming evidence is not the same as proof; always do the due diligence
- Qualify uncertain statements rather than overstating confidence
- Ignoring inconvenient evidence is never neutral — it is a choice with consequences
- Apply equal creative energy to alternatives as to your own preferred position
