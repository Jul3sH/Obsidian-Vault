# Decision Making Under Pressure

## Why Pressure Distorts Decisions
- Under time or emotional pressure, the brain defaults to the first plausible option and stops searching
- Panic, discomfort, and the desire to "just have it done" all feel like urgency but are actually noise
- Emotional decisions made on the spot are almost always suboptimal — the cost is paid later

## The Core Failure Modes

### Rushing action without preparation
- Acting fast to prove you are decisive (or not procrastinating) is not the same as acting well
- Trust your instinct — but verify it is your instinct, not an imitation of how you think someone else would act
- Preparation is not procrastination; the gut often signals *"I need to prepare more"* — that signal is worth heeding

### Panic + discomfort locking in bad outcomes
- Discomfort asking for more, or fear of losing a deal, can cause you to settle for worse terms than were available
- The fix: check the baseline (what does the market actually offer?) before committing
- Recognise that the discomfort of asking is temporary; the cost of a bad decision compounds over time

### Going down rabbit holes under pressure
- Problem-solving under pressure can create a cascade: solving the sub-problems of your chosen solution rather than revisiting whether the solution is right
- Periodically take a step back and ask: *"Are we still solving the actual problem in the most practical way?"*
- The best solution may be simpler than the one you are currently entangled in

### Not thinking through multi-party dynamics
- Decisions that involve multiple people require you to model how each person will react — not just the immediate recipient
- Think through the approach from several viewpoints before acting
- A message intended to give confidence can backfire if it creates unintended social dynamics between the other parties

## The Decision Framework

1. **Identify the actual problem** — not the symptoms, not the sub-problems of your current approach
2. **Check the baseline** — what does the market/evidence/alternative actually look like?
3. **Spot emotional interference** — is urgency or discomfort driving the decision?
4. **Use multiple viewpoints** — how will each stakeholder read this?
5. **Sanity-check your instinct** — trust it, but verify you are not telling yourself a convenient story
6. **Conscious cost-benefit** — when at a genuine crossroads, evaluate deliberately; don't drift

## Spot Checks
- When deep in problem-solving, stop periodically: *"Is this still the right approach?"*
- Ask: *"Am I solving the real problem or am I solving the problem created by my solution?"*
- Ask: *"Would I make this decision if I weren't under time/social/emotional pressure right now?"*

## My Pattern

Discomfort-as-urgency causes settling for worse terms. Rabbit-hole cascade in problem-solving: deeply invested in sub-problems without checking if the approach is still right. Multi-party dynamics often backfire.

## Key Takeaways
- Pressure creates a bias toward the first available option — consciously counteract this
- Discomfort is not urgency; distinguish between the two before acting
- Preparation is not procrastination — your instinct often signals genuine need for more groundwork
- Periodically step back during problem-solving to check you are still on the right track
- Model multi-party reactions before communicating — a well-intentioned move can backfire
- Deliberate cost-benefit beats unconscious drift every time
