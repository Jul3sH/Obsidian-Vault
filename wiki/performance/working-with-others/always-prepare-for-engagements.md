> *Every important interaction is an opportunity — preparation is what separates people who make impressions from people who just show up.*

## The core habit: always have a purpose
- Before any engagement — however brief — ask: "What is the point of this interaction?"
- Gather your thoughts, define your objective, then engage
- Without a clear purpose, interactions drift and opportunities are missed
- Even passing conversations with senior management need a prepared angle

## Have a best-practice method for every engagement type
- Workshops, interviews, one-to-ones, presentations, senior management encounters — each has a structure
- Build templates and methods for recurring engagement types so preparation is faster each time
- Improvising in important situations is a false economy

## Interview and pitch preparation
- Prioritise: look for what you can confidently work into the conversation (e.g. company background, specific product knowledge)
- Don't over-invest in areas that are hit-and-miss (exhaustive technical prep at the expense of the basics)
- Create "hooks" — answers you've steered towards a strength, going slightly beyond what was asked to display depth
- Identify the interviewer's or buyer's needs first — listen, then target your response to what they actually need
- Prepare specific questions to ask; they signal engagement and competence

## Seize opportunities for self-promotion
- Always be thinking about opportunities to be visible to the leadership team
- All-hands calls, Q&A sessions, senior stakeholder meetings — these are stages, not obligations
- Spotting the opportunity is step one; preparing a question or point in advance is step two

## Presentation discipline
- Per-slide storyline first — know what you're communicating on each slide before polishing it
- Get feedback and clarify requirements *before* getting into flow state on a polished version
- Force yourself to interrupt "flow" mode for checkpoint reviews — especially when presenting to an audience whose needs you haven't confirmed
- Simple language, clear structure, minimal iterations

## Getting feedback and interrupting flow
- Reluctance to get feedback (for fear of disrupting progress) is a trap — the correction cost later is always higher
- Build in checkpoints to validate content before deep refinement
- When in flow state on something that avoids emotional discomfort (e.g. polishing slides instead of drilling the hard content), notice it and course-correct

## Identifying opportunities to self-promote
- Review your work for moments where you've achieved something worth communicating upward
- These don't always announce themselves — actively scan for them
- A prepared question at an all-hands call; a well-timed email summarising a win; a voluntary contribution in a visible meeting

## Key Takeaways
- Every engagement has a purpose — define it before you start
- Build templates for recurring engagement types to reduce preparation time
- In interviews and pitches: identify their needs, then target your response
- Prepare hooks — pivot towards strengths, display depth beyond the question
- Get feedback early; don't polish something that might be going in the wrong direction
