> *Rework is always more expensive than the time it takes to get requirements right at the start.*

## The pattern
- Proceeding without clear requirements wastes far more time than pausing to establish them
- Ambiguity at the start becomes scope creep, rework, and blame later
- You will be held accountable for the output regardless of how vague the brief was

## It is your responsibility to clarify
- Don't wait for requirements to be handed to you — extract them
- You are expected to use your experience to ask the right questions and govern the scoping process
- If requirements can't be established, that itself is a risk to surface and document
- "The customer is always right" applies to *outcome* — but you own the process of establishing what that outcome is

## Before committing to any deliverable, define
- Objectives: what does success look like?
- Key Results / measurable outcomes (OKRs): how will we know we've succeeded?
- Constraints: time, budget, resource, scope limits
- Assumptions and dependencies
- What's explicitly out of scope

## Get sign-off, not just agreement
- Verbal agreement is not enough — confirm objectives in writing
- "Pre-approval from stakeholders before you start" is the rule when the required output is ambiguous
- If the brief changes, document and confirm the change; don't absorb scope creep silently

## Get buy-in before effort
- Run significant pieces of work by the key decision-maker *before* investing time in them
- "OK" from someone who doesn't really want it is not buy-in
- A brief check-in can save days of wasted work

## Validate priorities before starting
- Make sure you are working on what actually matters to the organisation right now
- Don't assume last week's priorities are still this week's priorities
- Validate with your manager: "Is this still the top priority given X?"

## Track objectives and changes
- Create and maintain a log of objectives, and update it when they change
- Use this as an audit trail — in political environments, people will rewrite history
- Before important meetings, review the objective history so you can identify contradictions

## OKRs as a forcing function
- Define objectives and key results for everything significant
- Once defined: prioritise → schedule → time-box
- Don't skip OKRs in favour of jumping straight into execution

## Key Takeaways
- Clarifying requirements upfront saves multiples of that time in rework
- You own the responsibility for getting the brief right — don't blame ambiguity
- Get written sign-off on objectives; track changes with an audit trail
- Get buy-in before effort, not after
- Validate priorities regularly — don't assume yesterday's priorities still hold

## My Pattern

The instinct to start delivering rather than hold the line on requirements is partly action-bias and partly relationship-driven. Political environments are highest-risk when scope changes without a written audit trail.
