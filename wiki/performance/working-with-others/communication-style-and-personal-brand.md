> *Your brand is what people say about you when you're not there — build it deliberately or it builds itself.*

## Succinctness is the baseline
- In Asia especially, key points must be communicated very succinctly — the gist will be missed if buried in context
- People want the answer, not the story (unless you are deliberately storytelling)
- Leave out descriptive narrative unless you have a specific reason to include it
- Always know your current status and be able to state it clearly and briefly

## Use future tense — be option-oriented
- Talking in future tense signals you're looking forward, not dwelling on what went wrong
- "Here's what we're going to do" lands better than "here's why it went wrong"
- Even when making a point about the past, frame it as a forward-looking option or improvement

## Deliberate silence is a communication tool
- Don't communicate preferences out loud when you should just be acting on them
- Talking about roadblocks gives the impression of navel-gazing, not leadership
- Deliberate silently — strategise internally, communicate decisions externally
- "Be comfortable with the silence" — strategic thinking doesn't need to be narrated

## Personal brand: define it or it defines you
- State your unique combination clearly so people can grasp it and tell others
- You need a purpose, aims, and goals that are crisp enough to be repeatable
- Your brand is what you want people to say when they recommend you — know what that is
- Be clear on it before every important engagement

## Elevator pitch and anecdotes
- Have a well-scripted elevator pitch — rehearsed and ready
- Have a set of polished anecdotes for networking situations — think about how they land *before* telling them
- Risky stories need scripting: consider how they could be misread before you tell them
- Don't tell a story about yourself that could be read as dishonest, chaotic, or self-aggrandising

## Always be ready to sell yourself
- Know your own value and be able to communicate it confidently at any moment
- Needy or self-deprecating behaviour in important conversations (especially with people who could help your career) leaves a bad impression
- Turn up sharp, bullish, and prepared — if you're not in the right state of mind, don't turn up

## Key Takeaways
- Be succinct: answer first, story second (or not at all)
- Use future tense to signal you're option-oriented and forward-looking
- Define your personal brand deliberately — know your unique combination and be able to state it clearly
- Have scripted anecdotes and an elevator pitch; always be ready to sell yourself
- Communicate decisions, not deliberations
