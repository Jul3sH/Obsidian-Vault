> *You are judged on whether the team delivers — not on which tasks you personally completed.*

## The core mindset shift
- Your job as a manager is delivery through the team, not delivery by yourself
- You will be judged on outcomes, not on which tasks you personally did
- Hoarding responsibility is a failure of leadership, not a sign of dedication

## Delegate decisively and early
- Don't wait until you understand everyone's workload before delegating — just do it
- Tell people what to do, don't ask — give more, not less
- Let them figure out the how; you define the what and what "good" looks like
- Delegate early enough that there is time to course-correct if needed

## Let them prioritise
- Load up your team; ask them to manage their own workloads and escalate blockers
- Use periodic status checks with a clear escalation process — don't try to track every task yourself
- Micromanaging signals you don't trust the team and creates bottlenecks

## Create feedback loops
- Set up periodic check-ins so that problems surface early
- Make accountability visible without making it oppressive
- When leadership is chaotic (constantly changing scope), create a feedback loop that surfaces resource and capacity constraints quickly

## Consultative, not collaborative, by default
- Don't run every decision past the team for consensus — it takes too long and signals indecision
- Instead: formulate a clear proposal, present it, ask for agreement or objections
- "I suggest the following approach… [bullets]… does anyone see issues?" — then it's one word to proceed
- Soliciting input is respectful; abdicating the decision is not

## Don't own other people's problems
- If a member of another team is underperforming, make it their manager's problem — not yours
- Creating overhead to compensate for someone else's weak team member is not your responsibility
- Be pleasant, but escalate quality issues to the right owner promptly

## Make management initiatives succeed
- If you've communicated an initiative to your manager, you must make it work
- Don't showcase something and then quietly drop it because it got hard
- Insist the team adopts new processes — being soft about it is not kindness, it's failure

## Leadership under pressure
- In a crunch, collaborate actively — pull in SMEs, get two back-to-back calls, build on each insight
- Don't try to do everything yourself; the breakthrough often comes from the second conversation
- People often don't know what to do, so they do nothing — tell them what to do and they'll work out how

## Leadership presence
- Always demonstrate leadership even when you're not formally in charge
- If you're the most senior person in the room, step up
- Don't sit back because you're not in the mood — people remember who led

## Key Takeaways
- You're measured on team delivery, not personal task completion
- Delegate early, delegate more — let people manage their own priorities and escalate
- Use consultative decision-making: propose → seek input → decide
- Don't absorb other teams' quality problems — make them visible and return them
- Leadership is an always-on posture, not a role you step into when called

## My Pattern

The instinct to retain tasks requires deliberate effort to shift. Consultative decision-making (propose → seek input → decide) reads as leadership; seeking consensus reads as indecision. Chaotic environments require clear delegation and escalation paths.
