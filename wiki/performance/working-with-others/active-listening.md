> *Listening is a skill that signals wisdom, builds rapport, and is rarer than talking.*

## The core failure modes
- Hearing the first few words, deciding you know the answer, then mentally composing your reply instead of listening
- Interrupting, finishing sentences, or pre-empting responses
- Elaborating on your question before the other person has had a chance to answer
- Answering the question you expected rather than the one actually asked
- Making the conversation about yourself

## Rules for active listening

### Answer the actual question
- Listen to the *whole* question before you begin to formulate a response
- If unsure what was asked — clarify, don't guess
- "Can you tell me a bit more about what you mean by X?" is never a weakness

### Ask questions, then shut up
- Ask a clear question, then give people time to respond — silence is not a misunderstanding
- Resist the urge to fill silence by elaborating further on the question
- Don't pre-empt their answer

### Never interrupt
- Interrupting and finishing others' sentences signals you value your own thoughts over theirs
- Even if you think you know what they're going to say — let them say it

### Get people, not yourself
- The goal in conversation is to understand *them*, not to display your knowledge or achievements
- Winners listen — salespeople talk; good salespeople listen
- Ask about them; keep your wisdom limited
- The person remembered as great (like Warren) was the one who always wanted to know about others and help them solve problems

### Don't make it about you
- Resist hijacking conversations to demonstrate how tough life is for you
- No one cares about your lost keys; drama about your own situation puts others off
- If it's a story about yourself — is it genuinely relevant, or is it seeking approval?

## Concise communication as a companion skill
- Leave out descriptive narrative unless you're deliberately storytelling
- People want the answer, not the journey to the answer
- Never repeat yourself — if they didn't react the way you hoped, it's either not important to them or you need a different approach (not more repetition)
- State the current status succinctly; long context-setting loses the room

## Key Takeaways
- Listen to the full question; answer what was actually asked
- Ask questions then go quiet — silence means they're thinking, not confused
- Never interrupt or finish sentences
- The conversation should be about them, not you
- Cut narrative filler; people want answers, not stories

See also: [[never-bitch-whinge-or-gossip]], [[influencing-and-selling-ideas]], [[communication-style-and-personal-brand]]

## My Pattern

The pull to make conversations about yourself pulls focus away from understanding what the other person actually needs. Answering the question expected rather than the one asked is a companion issue. Long context-setting before answers loses rooms.
