> *Delivery is the only currency that matters — smart, busy, and trying don't count.*

## The fundamental rule
- Deliver what your boss wants, not what you think is right or what you want to deliver
- If you disagree with the direction, push back once through the right channel — then deliver
- Nobody cares about how clever your ideas are; they care about outcomes

## Speed over perfection
- Speed of delivery is more important than perfection — perfection often goes unnoticed
- "Done ahead of schedule" impresses; a polished document delivered late does not
- If overwhelmed: ignore everything else, pick the single most important deliverable, and complete it
- You can justify ignoring three requests because you're delivering one — you cannot justify delivering nothing

## Proactive delivery (don't ask for permission)
- Senior people don't want to be consulted on obvious things — they want to be told you've done them
- "Don't ask permission, ask for forgiveness" — leaders are busy, make things happen
- Communicate in confident statements, not questions, about what you will be doing
- Operate with the assumption that you have the authority to act — they'll redirect you if you overstepped

## Deliver what was asked, not what you want to deliver
- Wanting to change things is fine — but you earn that right by first delivering what was requested
- Don't get frustrated that the corporate culture isn't what you want; get stuck in, prove your value, then make changes
- Looking good, being busy, and having good ideas are not results

## Demonstrating leadership through action
- Leaders don't wait to be told — they identify what needs doing and do it
- Reel off lists of things accomplished, not things planned
- "Self-starter" is the most valued trait at senior levels — embody it visibly

## Avoiding the trap of other priorities
- Politics, email perfection, and helping with other people's battles are diversions from delivery
- Stay focused on the day job; don't fight battles that aren't yours
- Results — not smartness, not looking good — are what get you noticed and promoted

## Key Takeaways
- Deliver what the boss wants, without fuss, on time — or early
- Speed beats perfection
- Act proactively; don't ask for permission on things within your remit
- Results are the currency — not ideas, effort, or appearance
- Earn the right to change things by delivering first

## My Pattern

Frustration with corporate culture and wanting change before earning credibility is a real pattern. Proactive delivery works with established authority; applying it prematurely is risky. Speed over perfection is the counter to perfectionism drift.
