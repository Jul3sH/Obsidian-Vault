> *People support ideas that benefit them — sell the outcome they want, not the idea you have.*

## Frame everything as "what's in it for them"
- Always ask: "Am I selling this in terms of what is in this for THEM, not me?"
- Bosses and stakeholders filter everything through their own objectives — speak to those
- Don't present your idea as your idea; present it as a solution to their problem

## Win-win over positional bargaining
- Positional bargaining wastes creative energy defending a position
- Redirect that energy to making the other person's suggestion work — often it's fine
- Getting to yes together is more durable than winning the argument

## Never tell people they are wrong
- However pissed off you are, never look like you're telling someone they're wrong — it just creates an argument you can't win
- Suggest solutions; don't criticise the current approach
- Frame corrections as "options to solve the problem together"
- Allow people to save face — they are far more likely to change course if they don't feel embarrassed

## Encouraging over criticising
- Criticism creates defensiveness; encouragement creates movement
- Set objectives, back off, and check in with a positive tone — don't micromanage
- Ask "how are we doing?" not "why haven't you done X?"

## HTWFAIP principles in practice (How To Win Friends And Influence People)
- Apply consistently — they work
- Give people a reputation to live up to
- Let the other person feel the idea is theirs

## "Yes to the person, no to the task"
- Maintain the relationship even when declining or pushing back on the work
- Disagree with the request, not the person
- Apologies for *how* you handled a situation (not necessarily that you raised it) to maintain goodwill

## Choose battles wisely
- Fighting losing battles — especially in office politics — drains credibility and energy
- Ask: what do I realistically expect to achieve here?
- If it's a battle you can't win, don't start it
- Get the right people on side to carry your message rather than fighting directly

## Collaboration is strength, not weakness
- Asking for ideas and input is collaboration — not an admission you don't know the answer
- Brainstorming with others early surfaces problems and builds buy-in simultaneously

## Key Takeaways
- Always sell in terms of their benefit, not yours
- Never tell someone they're wrong — suggest a solution instead
- Win-win beats positional bargaining every time
- Allow people to save face; they'll follow your lead without losing credibility
- Pick battles you can win; build allies for the ones you can't fight alone

## My Pattern

The battle-choosing principle is especially relevant: investing energy in fights that can't be won — particularly in politically charged organisations — drains credibility and creates adversaries. The reframe of "never tell people they're wrong" is a useful correction to a natural pattern: suggesting the alternative as a solution to a shared problem works better than pointing out error.
