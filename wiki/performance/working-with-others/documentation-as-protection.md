> *In political environments, if it isn't written down it didn't happen — documentation is self-defence.*

## Why documentation matters
- When things go wrong, people rewrite history to protect themselves
- Without an audit trail, you cannot defend your position — even if you were right
- Documentation forces clarity on objectives, actions, and responsibilities before they become contentious
- It's not paranoia; it's professional practice

## What to document
- Objectives and key results for every significant deliverable — signed off, not just discussed
- Changes to objectives: every scope change should be confirmed in writing with the date
- Meeting actions: who agreed to do what, by when
- Constraints and risks raised: if you flagged it, prove you flagged it
- Escalations: if you raised an issue, document that you raised it

## Meeting notes are a priority — write them the same day
- By the next day, detail is lost and your notes are unreadable
- It's a false economy to delay — it costs more time and stress to reconstruct later
- Never procrastinate on meeting notes; treat it as categorically higher priority than it feels in the moment

## Handovers must be watertight
- Document everything being handed over: processes, outstanding items, ownership
- Get the recipient to review and sign off the handover document
- A handover without documentation is not a handover — it's an undefended liability

## Accurate communication is your responsibility
- Even if your manager doesn't ask for written updates — send them anyway
- It covers you if information is relayed inaccurately by others
- "I sent an update on X date confirming Y" is a powerful position to be in

## Create evaluation reports
- For significant deliverables, create a report that documents: objectives, approach, constraints, outcomes
- This is both a delivery record and a capability showcase
- Use deliverables trackers: objectives stated, requirements documented, constraints listed, changes tracked

## Visibly raise issues early
- When alarm bells ring, surface the issue immediately — don't wait for a crisis
- Raise it through the agreed escalation path so it's on record
- The moment you document an issue, the responsibility shifts from personal to organisational

## CSFs and KPIs for core responsibilities
- For ongoing roles (not just projects), define Critical Success Factors and track KPIs
- Report progress against these regularly — it demonstrates you are evaluating your own performance
- Makes it very hard for others to claim you weren't delivering

## Key Takeaways
- Documentation is not bureaucracy — it is protection in political environments
- Write meeting notes the same day; never delay
- Get objectives and changes confirmed in writing; always maintain an audit trail
- Handovers must be documented and signed off
- Raise issues early and visibly — if you escalated it, document that you did
