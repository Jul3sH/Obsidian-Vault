> *Bosses want to hear what you've solved, not what you're struggling with — manage up deliberately and relentlessly.*

## The core principle
- Bosses want results, not progress reports
- They're not interested in problems unless you've already done everything you can to resolve them
- Communicate success, not effort; outcomes, not activity

## Bring solutions, not problems
- Never bring a problem without a proposed solution or mitigation
- Proactively manage risk: identify it early, propose a response, then raise it
- If it has to be escalated, show you've exhausted every option within your authority first
- Don't create drama — drama signals you're not in control

## Managing up as a priority (not an afterthought)
- Managing upward is a core responsibility — not a nice-to-have
- Senior management's perception of you is largely shaped by how you communicate with them
- Make it a habit to be clear in your own mind about your current status before any interaction with senior management

## What to communicate and how
- Short, sweet, succinct, factual, pragmatic statements — not "look what I've done"
- Only tell senior management things that have a clear point — make the point obvious
- Frame updates as: "Here is what I have already done to move us to the next step" (not "here is what I've been up to")
- Use future tense to sound option-oriented: it signals you're already looking ahead
- Never slip into small talk with senior management unless it's scripted and rehearsed

## Visibility and transparency
- Issues need to be visible — if problems aren't surfaced, facts get distorted by others
- Sell ideas to your immediate chain; get visible agreement; make it their responsibility to communicate upward
- Document and make explicit — if it's not written down, it didn't happen

## Scripted small talk with senior management
- Unplanned small talk with directors is a risk, not an opportunity — unless you're prepared
- Resist the urge to mention problems in passing; it comes across as lacking control
- When tired or emotionally charged, think before speaking to senior management

## In disorganised/political environments
- When leadership is chaotic, the blame game starts early — cover yourself immediately when alarm bells ring
- Visibly escalate issues early; waiting until it's a crisis leaves you exposed
- Agree an escalation path at the start of projects — then follow it transparently

## Always have clear communication objectives
- Before any communication, ask: what is the objective of this message?
- If there's no clear objective, don't send it
- Every update, email, or conversation should move something forward

## Key Takeaways
- Bosses want solutions and results — never bring a problem without a proposed fix
- Communicate success, not progress or effort
- Managing up is a proactive skill — make it a scheduled priority
- Keep updates short, factual, future-oriented
- Escalate issues early and visibly — don't wait for the crisis

## My Pattern

Managing up proactively requires scheduling discipline. Raising concerns but folding when met with mild resistance is a known pattern. Unscripted small talk with senior management when tired or frustrated undermines the impression built in prepared interactions.
