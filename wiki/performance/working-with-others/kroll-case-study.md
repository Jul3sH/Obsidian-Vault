> *A textbook example of what happens when can-do attitude overrides proper evaluation, requirements gathering, and assertive pushback.*

See also: [[career/_index]]

## Context
- Engagement at Computacenter (CC) for KROLL, a new client — an infrastructure strategy document
- Constraints from the outset: no real requirements, described internally as "basically a PR exercise" by the account manager (Steve Davies)
- Time allocation: 3 days (later contested to 4), no formal Qualification Review in place
- Julian was new to the organisation, keen to build a pipeline and display a can-do attitude

## What happened

### Pre-engagement
- Assigned after last-minute availability change (Riz unavailable) — one-day workshop
- Prepared an EOL assessment and requested confirmation of infrastructure details; received no response
- Ran a 3-hour workshop — customer was clearly disengaged, non-committal, and unable to articulate requirements
- Post-workshop: sales (Rick) immediately began requesting design deliverables, implying 4 hours of design from a 3-hour meeting

### During the engagement
- Attempted multiple times to qualify requirements — received vague or no responses
- Escalated ambiguity internally; sought mentoring from Ian (process guidance) and engaged Mark and Alan
- Suggested an alternative approach (C3/private cloud angle) hoping to give the customer something concrete — this crossed into other teams' territory and created political risk
- Managed to stabilise expectations: agreed to deliver a greenfields strategy paper with clear caveats
- Discovered process gap: work of this type should have gone through a formal Qualification Review and Pricing Review — Chris Davies (internal sales) had not followed the proper process

### Communication of concerns
- Raised concerns clearly with Mark; Mark confirmed the ambiguity but essentially said "manage it as best you can"
- Julian was candid about discomfort but overcame it too easily — he proceeded when the right answer was to push back harder

## Where it went wrong
- **Took on a deliverable with too many constraints** without properly evaluating whether the output could be worthwhile
- **No clear requirements** — the customer couldn't articulate what they wanted, and the brief never improved
- **Did not push back assertively enough** on the lack of requirements or the process gap (no QR)
- **Can-do attitude without risk assessment** — being new and keen to build a pipeline led to absorbing a deliverable that others had correctly declined
- **Forecasting was unstructured** — time estimates made without listing requirements, constraints, and dependencies first
- **Crossed into other teams' territory** (IS&I, C3 teams) without sufficiently managing the political implications
- **Didn't get into flow state on the right things** — spent time on approach iterations instead of forcing the requirements conversation earlier

## Lessons extracted
- [[assertiveness-and-risk-dont-over-commit]]: Evaluate fully before committing. The risk of delivering something worthless outweighs the benefit of looking keen
- [[clarify-objectives-before-committing]]: No requirements = no deliverable. Make this explicit and push the problem back to the account team
- [[deliver-what-your-boss-wants]]: Know exactly what the output should be before forecasting effort — list requirements, deliverables, and constraints first
- [[documentation-as-protection]]: If processes haven't been followed (no QR), document this and escalate before starting work — not after
- [[managing-up-bring-solutions-communicate-success]]: Raised concerns but folded too quickly; the correct escalation was to refuse to proceed without a formal brief
- [[internal-networking-and-relationship-building]]: Being new and wanting to build a pipeline is understandable — but it must not override professional judgement on whether a deliverable is feasible

## My Pattern

This is a direct personal experience at Computacenter. Being new to the organisation and wanting to build a pipeline overrode professional judgement about whether the deliverable was viable. The pattern of absorbing a bad brief rather than pushing it back — because saying no felt like a risk to the relationship — shows up in other contexts too. The key failure was proceeding when multiple signals (no requirements, disengaged customer, process gap, internal description as "basically a PR exercise") all pointed to the same answer: this should not have been taken on without a formal brief.

## Key Takeaways
- "Can-do" without proper evaluation is a liability, not an asset
- No requirements = no commitment; push the problem back to the people who can solve it
- Being new is not a reason to absorb bad briefs — it's a reason to ask more questions, not fewer
- Forecast effort *after* listing requirements, constraints, and deliverables — never before
- If the process hasn't been followed, document it and escalate immediately; don't absorb the risk silently
- The cost of saying no upfront is almost always less than the cost of failing to deliver
