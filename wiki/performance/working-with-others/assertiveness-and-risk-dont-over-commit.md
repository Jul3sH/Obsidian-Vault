> *Can-do attitude is only an asset when you've assessed the risk — over-committing is just a slow-motion failure.*

## The failure mode: enthusiasm without evaluation
- Taking on deliverables with too many constraints, unclear requirements, or inadequate resourcing to display "can-do"
- The gain from looking keen is almost always less than the cost of failing to deliver
- Ask before committing: "What am I actually hoping to achieve by taking this on?"
- If the answer isn't clear, don't take it on

## Evaluate before committing
- List: requirements, constraints, deliverables, effort, dependencies
- If anything is missing, flag it as a risk *before* agreeing
- Forecast realistically — then add tolerance for what you don't know
- If you can't deliver what's being asked in the timeframe, say so clearly and early; silence is worse

## Assertiveness is not making a fuss
- Saying no, negotiating, and pushing back are professional rights — not weakness
- People see a walkover as weak, not super-efficient or helpful
- Being assertive about requirements, resourcing, and timelines is doing your job
- If forced to go against best practice, escalate immediately — it takes less time than retrospectively dealing with the failure

## When to push back
- Insufficient requirements to produce quality output
- Inadequate resource (people, time, budget) for what's been committed
- Scope expanding beyond what was originally agreed
- Process or authority not in place to complete the work properly

## Analyse stakeholders first
- Establish who you can and can't say no to
- For those you can push back on: be assertive and do so
- For those with more authority: frame pushback as protecting the shared outcome, not refusing to help
- "I'm genuinely concerned we won't deliver what's needed" lands better than "that's not possible"

## Resourcing is a priority to surface early
- Raise resource constraints early — as soon as you're aware of them
- Once the request is made and documented, the resource problem belongs to the organisation, not just you
- Don't wait until failure is inevitable before asking for help; it's too late by then

## Agree an escalation path at the start
- Before any project or significant engagement, agree how issues will be escalated
- When the time comes to escalate, you're following agreed protocol — not making trouble
- This protects everyone and removes the political sting from raising concerns

## Proactively manage risk
- Identify risks early, propose mitigations, and communicate them
- Don't just identify the risk — bring a proposed response
- Mitigation documented and communicated protects you if the risk materialises

## Key Takeaways
- Evaluate fully before committing — "can-do" without a risk assessment is reckless
- Assertiveness (negotiating, pushing back) is professional, not difficult
- Surface resource and requirement gaps early — silence on these is dangerous
- Agree escalation paths upfront; then use them without apology
- The goal is to deliver — not to look willing

## My Pattern

The can-do instinct regularly wins over the evaluation instinct. Concerns raised softly then folded when met with mild resistance. Assertiveness is professional, not difficult, but requires repeated reinforcement.
