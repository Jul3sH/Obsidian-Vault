> *Proximity and relationships are the invisible infrastructure of career progression — treat them as a deliverable.*

## Why internal networking is non-negotiable
- Career progression in large organisations is built on relationships, not just results
- Being physically present and visible to the right people creates opportunities that remote or siloed working kills
- The "go-to person" in a team gets the stretch assignments, the credit, and the promotions
- Gio's promotion case study: cultivating the right relationship with the right person at the right time was the decisive factor — not just technical skill

## Physical presence matters
- Sit near the people who matter — proximity enables informal relationship-building
- If someone is becoming the "go-to" for your boss, move closer — the window closes fast
- Waiting to be discovered is not a strategy; you need to be there to be in the game

## Who to network with
- Network *wisely* early on: identify the key influential figures before you start forming allegiances
- Avoid forming bonds with people held in low regard — you'll be tarred with the same brush
- Back the right horse: don't invest heavily in someone whose opinion is not respected or who is about to be replaced
- Power shifts; build relationships across the organisation, not just with your immediate boss

## How to build relationships
- Invest time consistently — don't just engage when you need something
- Find what the other person cares about and speak to that (see [[influencing-and-selling-ideas]])
- Agree to calls or conversations that let the other person show their expertise — even if you don't need it
- After a period of confrontation, actively look for ways to smooth the relationship; don't let it fester

## Allies create power
- You need allies to build a power base — avoid unnecessary confrontation
- Keep enemies close: stay assertive but explain your reasoning; avoid escalating tensions
- Sending a snotty email vs. having a call — the call almost always preserves the relationship
- If someone is a potential foe, invest in the relationship before you need it

## Network strategically, not indiscriminately
- Don't give people ammunition through [[never-bitch-whinge-or-gossip|bitching or moaning]] during early networking
- Be pleasant and professional, but don't reveal your hand
- Every interaction is a data point about your reputation

## The "go-to" asset
- Being the person others come to for answers is one of the most powerful positions in an organisation
- It requires visibility, reliability, and consistent helpfulness over time
- Gio achieved this through proximity to Stuart, self-promotion, and delivering what Stuart needed

## Key Takeaways
- Relationships are a career asset that require active maintenance — not just a byproduct of good work
- Physical proximity to decision-makers matters more than most people acknowledge
- Back the right people; avoid aligning too early with those of low influence
- Build allies before you need them — especially in politically charged environments
- Be the go-to person: present, reliable, and visible to the right people

See also: [[never-bitch-whinge-or-gossip]], [[influencing-and-selling-ideas]], [[communication-style-and-personal-brand]], [[kroll-case-study]]

## My Pattern

The "back the right horse" principle has direct relevance — investing heavily in a manager who lacks genuine organisational standing means your credibility doesn't transfer when they leave or are replaced. The pattern of investing in relationships only when something is needed, rather than maintaining them consistently, is a known gap: relationships built from scratch in a moment of need are less effective than those maintained over time.
