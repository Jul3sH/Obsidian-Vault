# Drafted Response to Wendy's Contribution Request

> Purpose: a reply to Wendy's repeated requests that Julian "contribute" to Felix's medical/living costs, drafted to **decline clearly without creating any legal exposure**. Grounded in the documented position in [[dad-finance-discussions]] and the strategy in [[relationship-dynamics]] / [[wendy-profile]].
>
> **This is a draft for Julian's review — not legal advice.** Given the lawyer involvement already flagged (6 Feb 2026), have a solicitor review before sending if the stakes warrant it.

## The objective

Decline to contribute in a way that:
1. Makes **no commitment** — open-ended or specific, present or future.
2. Contains **no admission** that Julian has ever offered, agreed, or is obliged to contribute.
3. Creates **no implied willingness** ("if I could, I would") that could be construed as conditional agreement.
4. States the **factual basis** for declining: Wendy controls Felix's finances; Felix has his own assets and insurance; Felix expressly said he did not want to burden Julian.
5. Stays **calm, brief, and non-hostile** — no insults, no accusations, nothing that becomes useful evidence for the other side.
6. Does **not** deny caring about Felix — separates care from money.

## Drafting principles (why each choice matters)

- **Do not say "I can't afford it."** That implies you *would* contribute if you could — a conditional acceptance. Ground the refusal in **principle and fact** (Felix's own assets + Wendy's control), not in your means.
- **Do not negotiate an amount or ask "how much?"** Engaging with quantum implies the principle is conceded.
- **Do not agree "in principle" or "if it comes to it."** This is the exact open-ended commitment that could later be asserted against you.
- **State facts, not accusations.** "Felix has assets and insurance intended for this" is a fact and a defence. "You're lying about the money" is an accusation that escalates and gives her ammunition.
- **Reference Felix's own stated wish** that he did not want to burden Julian — this is documented and reframes refusal as honouring Felix.
- **Keep it short.** Long, emotional messages create more surface area for the other side to quote selectively.
- **Put nothing in writing you would not want read aloud in court.** Assume Wendy's lawyers will see the group chat (Julian's own 6 Feb 2026 assessment).

## Additional arguments (added after Julian's 27 May 2026 input)

These strengthen the refusal by removing Julian from the decision chain entirely — which is the best defence against any claim that he was "part of" a decision to incur cost and should therefore share it.

**1. The care decisions — and their funding — are Wendy's alone.**
- Felix has been in a **private ward**. Wendy chose the level of care and manages the insurance and finances.
- The level of care is a decision within *her* control as the person managing his finances. If cost were the constraint, the choice of ward sits with her.
- Whether the estate tops up the insurance shortfall is **her decision** — she is the sole executor and the beneficiary who inherits the estate. It has no bearing on Julian. The cost of choices she makes and funds cannot become Julian's liability.

**2. Wendy has already demonstrated Julian is not a decision-maker.**
- Before the most recent operation she solicited opinions. **Both Julian and Hilary advised against prolonging**, given Felix's poor quality of life. Julian then asked Wendy what *she* thought.
- **She did not respond, and proceeded anyway** — disregarding the input she had asked for.
- This matters legally: it establishes that Julian has **no decision-making role** in Felix's care. He cannot be "part of" decisions he was excluded from and that were taken against his stated view.

**3. The "flip" — neutralise the lawyer trap.**
- The risk: Wendy elicits a response that can be characterised as Julian participating in a decision to incur costly care, then argues he should bear part of the cost.
- The counter: make explicit, in writing, that **decisions on Felix's care are hers and have been taken without — and against — Julian's input**, so Julian neither directs that care nor funds it.
- **Tone caution:** the *substance* of "if she's exceeded the insurance that's her own choice" is correct and protective. But avoid blame words like "her own fault" or "makes no difference to me" in writing — they read as callous and feed a "doesn't care about his father" narrative. State it as: *these are decisions for you as the person managing his finances and his estate.* Same point, no exposure.

**4. Hong Kong law — preliminary research done; supports the refusal.**
- Julian's preliminary research (see [[hong-kong-filial-liability]]) indicates **no general HK statute makes an adult child liable** for a parent's medical/care bills. Liability sits with the patient/debtor and, on death, the estate — reinforced by Felix having a spouse and his own property.
- **Real exposure comes from documents Julian might sign** (hospital admission forms, guarantees, care-home contracts) or misuse of funds as attorney/guardian. → **Never sign anything** that accepts payment responsibility.
- **Still do not assert "I have no legal obligation under HK law" in writing to Wendy** until a HK solicitor confirms it — asserting an unverified legal position could backfire. The factual/principled refusal stands on its own without it.

## Lawyer's Review of Draft C (contribution-defence lens)

> Reviewed solely for one risk: could anything here be used to argue Julian agreed to contribute, or made himself a co-decision-maker on costly care so as to share liability? Note: Julian is **not using a solicitor** (cost) — so the message itself is the only safeguard. That raises the bar: assert no legal position, concede nothing.

**What Draft C gets right**
- Unequivocal refusal ("will not be doing so").
- Refuses on principle/fact, not need.
- Records that Wendy solicited then overrode input → supports "not a decision-maker."
- Reaffirms care for Dad.

**Three redlines to fix:**

1. **"Before I respond, please can you clarify exactly what you are asking me for?" — move or cut.** Leading with this implies the response (possibly including a contribution) is *pending* her clarification. Make the refusal unconditional and lead with it. (You can still ask her to put her ask in writing — but *after* the refusal, framed so it's clearly not contingent.)

2. **"we did not think Dad's situation should be prolonged" — do not re-state your clinical opinion.** The defensive value is showing you were *overridden* — you can get that by saying you "gave your views" without repeating what they were. Repeating a treatment opinion re-inserts you into the care decision, which is the exact hook she wants. State that views were given and ignored; don't give a fresh one.

3. **"I am not in a position to contribute" — cut "in a position to."** It hints at circumstance ("…if my position changed, I might"). Say plainly: **"I will not be contributing."**

**Biggest live trap (outside this message):** her standing invitations — *"I welcome all constructive suggestions and ideas"* (29 Apr 2026) and *"I would appreciate any review and advise on next steps"* (30 May 2026). **Do not give care advice or endorse a placement/treatment in writing.** Offering an opinion on next steps = participating in the decision = her basis to argue you co-owned a costly choice. Decline to direct care; defer it to her as the manager/executor.

**On grounding her authority (important):** the sentence rests her decision-making on her **functional role** — she manages Dad's care and finances day to day — *not* solely on "previous decisions." Resting it only on precedent is circular ("she decides because she's decided before") and could be flipped ("you gave opinions too"). Functional control is the unassailable basis and can't be turned back on Julian. "As previous decisions have shown" stays only as secondary support.

**On Power of Attorney — deliberately NOT in the message.** Dad never formally assigned POA; Wendy acts by default (Julian's understanding). Tempting to cite, but raising it would (a) open a capacity/authority/guardianship dispute — a far bigger fight than "I'm not paying"; and (b) run into the one real HK liability trap (exposure comes from taking on a *formal* attorney/guardian role). For the contribution-defence goal Julian doesn't need to establish *why* Wendy has authority — only that he has *no* role and she runs the care and money. The no-POA fact is documented in the evidence file (see [[dad-finance-discussions]], Oct 2023) but kept out of any message to Wendy.

**Why the closing keeps the "I care" line but drops "keep me updated":** the update request was removed as redundant (Wendy messages Julian anyway and he visits in person). The closing sentence "I care about Dad very much and want him comfortable and well looked after" is **retained deliberately** — after a message that declines both the decision-making role and the contribution, an explicit statement of care is the guard against any "Julian doesn't care" characterisation. It commits to nothing and should not be cut.

**Why "insurance" was removed from the refusal line:** earlier drafts said "assets, arrangements and insurance." Insurance is exhausted and does not cover rehabilitation/convalescent care, so asserting it is **factually contestable** — it would hand Wendy a rebuttal ("there's no insurance for his care") and risk turning a clean refusal into a factual dispute. The refusal is strongest resting only on the **indisputable** facts: Dad has his own assets and Wendy manages them. "Arrangements" stays (vague, safe); "insurance" is out. General principle: never anchor the refusal on a fact she can argue.

**Decision rule for the non-contribution line:** include it **whenever her message touches cost or funding** (the 30 May "next steps" message does — rehab fees, uninsured). Drop it **only** if a message is purely medical with no money in it; then stay consistent by simply not raising money yourself. Avoid "As I've said" — state it as calm fact, not a reminder.

**Why Draft D is safe:**
- **Declines the decision role explicitly** ("I'm not in a position to direct those choices") — defuses the "you advised, so you co-decided" trap from 29 Apr / 30 May.
- **"I trust you to act in his best interests"** sounds supportive but commits Julian to *nothing* — it endorses no specific treatment, placement, or spend.
- **Restates the contribution refusal** in the same breath, kept consistent with 6 Feb 2026.
- **Warm and caring throughout** — cannot be read as indifference.
- Gives **no opinion on any option**, so there is no "costly choice Julian backed."

**Do NOT, in reply to a "next steps" request:**
- Name or rank any care home, rehab centre, or treatment.
- Say "I think X is better than Y" / "that one looks good" / "sounds sensible."
- Say "do whatever it takes" or "spare no expense" (endorses unlimited spend).
- Ask "how much will it cost?" (engages on quantum).
- Offer to "look into options" or "help find somewhere" (steps into the decision).

## What to avoid saying (anti-patterns)

| Don't say | Why it's dangerous |
|-----------|--------------------|
| "I can't afford it right now." | Implies willingness once you can — conditional acceptance. |
| "Maybe I can help with a bit." / "Let me see what I can do." | Open-ended commitment — exactly what to avoid. |
| "How much are you asking for?" | Engaging on quantum concedes the principle. |
| "If it really comes to it, I'll chip in." | A future promise that can be asserted later. |
| "You've stolen his money / you're lying." | Accusation; escalates; becomes her evidence; risks defamation framing. |
| "I'll contribute if Dad asks me himself." | Still a conditional agreement. |
| Long emotional account of the history. | More material to quote selectively; muddies the clear refusal. |
| "If you've exceeded the insurance, that's your own fault." | Correct in substance but reads as callous — feeds a "doesn't care" narrative. Say "these are your decisions to make" instead. |
| "It makes no difference to me whatsoever." | Sounds indifferent to Dad's care; quotable as not caring. The point (his funding is independent of you) can be made factually without it. |

## Key Takeaways
- Decline on **principle and fact** (Felix's assets + Wendy's control + Felix's wish), never on affordability.
- Make **no commitment, no admission, no conditional willingness** — these are the things that create future liability.
- Stay calm and factual; assume lawyers will read it; keep it short.
- Maintain consistency with every prior message; never soften later.
- Two ready drafts above (A concise, B firmer); have a solicitor review before sending if warranted.
