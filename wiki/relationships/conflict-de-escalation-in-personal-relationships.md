> *Most arguments are not about what they appear to be about.*

tags: [relationships]

## The Root Cause Principle

Positional bargaining — two people arguing from fixed positions — almost never resolves conflict. It escalates it. The real issue is usually an underlying feeling (neglect, insecurity, fear) that neither party has named.

Before negotiating, identify the root cause.

## Finding the Root Cause

- Ask: what is this person actually feeling that has produced this position?
- Common roots in personal conflict: feeling neglected, feeling unheard, feeling disrespected, feeling scared
- Address the underlying feeling first — often the presenting argument dissolves entirely once it's acknowledged
- Example: a dispute about going out with friends resolved at the root was about the partner feeling neglected. Address that; the practical objection disappears

## Responding to Criticism

- When criticised, the instinct is to defend or counter-attack. Both escalate
- Instead: absorb the criticism calmly, respond to the substance rather than the emotion behind the delivery
- If the criticism is indirect or implied, don't engage with the implication — wait until it's stated explicitly, then respond with calm counter-comments
- Criticising back ("you criticise too much") produces the exact outcome you're objecting to — it is itself criticism

## Never Mirror Aggression

- Matching an angry tone amplifies the conflict; it does not demonstrate strength
- Calm, composed responses in the face of aggression communicate more authority than matching the energy
- See also: [[working-with-yourself/never-react-walk-away-and-be-calculated]]

## What Not to Do

- Don't criticise — it achieves nothing and produces defensiveness. This is basic people-skills
- Don't try to win by getting compliance without genuine understanding — you'll get a resentful agreement, not a resolved conflict
- Don't escalate when your message hasn't landed — back off and return differently

## My Pattern

Positional bargaining on surface issues without identifying underlying feelings is a real pattern. Mirroring aggression amplifies rather than resolves. The criticising-for-criticising loop produces the exact dynamic it objects to.

## Key Takeaways

- Find the root cause before engaging on positions; the presenting argument is rarely the real issue
- Respond to criticism calmly and without counter-attack
- Never mirror aggression — composure is the stronger position
- Criticising someone for criticising is counterproductive; model the behaviour instead
- See also: [[communicating-in-close-relationships]], [[working-with-yourself/emotional-resilience-recover-from-setbacks]]
