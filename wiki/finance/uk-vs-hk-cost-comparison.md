## Method (three honesty rules)

1. **Compare the whole household, both properties, in each scenario.** Julian owns *both* homes. In either scenario he lives in one and lets the other. Comparing only "London housing" against "HK housing" is the error that inflated the saving - it ignored the Cecil Road rent he would collect if he stayed.
2. **Actuals vs estimates are not the same currency.** HK figures are 12-month *actuals* (they capture lumpy spend - holidays, one-offs). London figures are *estimates*. Comparing HK-actual to London-estimate silently flatters London by ~£900/mo of "invisible" lumpy spend London would also have. Corrected with a London lumpy buffer.
3. **Hold discretionary at HK-equivalent unless there is a genuine price reason.** No assumed "I'll live more simply in London" downshift. Dining stays at your HK level; only lines with a real structural price difference move.

*(FX held at the model's 10:1 HKD:GBP.)*

## 4. Why it *felt* like "half price" (and wasn't)

The earlier version of [[london-monthly-budget|London Monthly Budget]] showed London at £4,427 vs HK ~£8,400 - implying a ~£4k/mo saving. Two errors, both now corrected:

2. It assumed London **discretionary** (dining/groceries) would drop - a lifestyle downshift, not a price difference. Your "feels the same price" instinct correctly caught this.

Your skepticism was well-founded. The current saving is only roughly ~£300-400/mo before car / Clodagh / one-offs, not ~£4,000.

## 6. What this means for the decision (honest)

This does **not** overturn the move - but it does move the weight, exactly where you said it should go:

- **The cost saving is small and fragile, and it is NOT a load-bearing pillar of the move.** School-fees-and-a-bit is useful, but it does not by itself justify uprooting.
- **Therefore your Point 1 gains force: the decision leans more heavily on P(landing suitable London work) + the tax differential than a "London is much cheaper" story would.** A modest cost saving means HK's lower tax on an equivalent salary matters *more*, not less. If Test B shows London job odds are **not** materially better, your "HK wins on tax" logic has real teeth.
- **BUT the load-bearing pillar was never the cost saving.** It was: (a) P(landing a *senior* HK role - the only kind that covers the HK burn) is below breakeven on your own estimate; (b) the move's downside is *capped* by ~4 years of runway; (c) staying-and-drifting is the worst cell. Test A leaves those intact. It just correctly demotes "cheaper" from a reason to a footnote, and **promotes Test B (the job market) to the real swing factor.**

Net: Test A firms up one assumption honestly (London is *somewhat* cheaper, driven by school), kills an overstatement (not half-price, housing neutral), and hands the decisive weight to Test B. That is what a good stress-test does.
