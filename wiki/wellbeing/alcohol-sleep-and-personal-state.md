## Alcohol: The Hidden Tax

Alcohol rarely announces itself as the problem. Its costs accumulate quietly:

- **Paranoia and risk aversion** — drinking consistently produces a low-grade paranoid state. Paranoia kills risk-taking, progress, and achievement
- **Physical deterioration** — fatigue, poor appearance, reduced sharpness. These compound into a general state of "past it" before the cause is identified
- **Financial damage** — impaired judgement leads to poor decisions. One real example: failing to do due diligence on an employment contract cost approximately £8k because drinking had made the habit of cutting corners feel normal
- **Lazy thinking** — the cost isn't just the hangover day; it's the slow erosion of standard and rigour over time

## Maintaining Control in Public

- Never lose visible control, even at social events — once people see you drunk, they question your self-discipline, willpower, and judgement
- Drunk conversations with important people (colleagues, senior contacts, clients) carry disproportionate reputational risk — embarrassing stories travel
- **Rule:** make a strong impression, then move on. Only elaborate when asked. Don't overstay the moment
- If drinking socially, mentally rehearse what you're about to say before saying it

## Sleep

- Sleep deprivation produces paranoia, reduced performance, and poor decisions — the same state as too much alcohol
- Being judged on results, not time-in-seat, means protecting sleep is professional — not lazy
- Don't be a martyr about early starts and punishing schedules. Consistent output requires consistent recovery

## Work-Play Separation

- Unfinished work hanging over rest ruins both the rest and relationships
- The correct sequence: push hard, finish the work completely, then relax fully — with full attention given to family and recovery
- Half-resting while mentally still working gives you the cost of both without the benefit of either

## My Pattern

Alcohol makes cutting corners feel normal and suppresses risk-taking clarity. The paranoia effect accumulates quietly. Work-play separation is critical: mental half-presence in both means neither gets proper attention.

## Key Takeaways

- Alcohol is subtle and cumulative — its real costs (paranoia, laziness, poor judgement, financial mistakes) are easy to rationalise away
- Never be visibly drunk around important people; the reputational damage is asymmetric
- Sleep is a professional asset — protect it without guilt
- Full recovery requires clean separation from work; partial presence in both states is the worst outcome
