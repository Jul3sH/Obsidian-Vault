## Bottom line

1. **£150k+ gross is achievable, but it is a top-decile (P90) outcome in every verified lane, not the expected result.** The honest central case for this profile is **perm £85-130k base / contract ~£120-145k salary-equivalent net-of-benefits**. Clearing the £150k floor requires specifically the presales-OTE lane or the BFSI / consultancy top-of-band lane. It is an upside scenario, not the base case.
2. **Contract does NOT beat a £150k permanent package once benefits are counted.** This is the single most decision-relevant finding, and it reverses the "chase a higher-paid contract" instinct. Detail below.
3. **The market is a mild headwind, not a tailwind:** vacancy volume is up, but London advertised pay is flat-to-declining.
4. **Age cannot carry the relocation.** The UK-vs-HK age delta is real legally but unproven on hiring outcomes. It is a legitimate *secondary* argument; the profile's framing of it as "the core mover" is over-stated. The verifiable anchor for the move is **market size and right-to-work**, not age.

## Two bars: "cover my costs" (floor) vs "£150k well-paid" (target)

This report was scoped to the **£150k well-paid target** - the *ceiling*, which is hard (top-decile). The **cost-covering floor** is a different, much easier bar, and the two invert in difficulty. There are two useful versions of "cover my costs":

- **Net-worth break-even:** income covers real consumption and non-equity costs. The bank balance may still move cash into the DB mortgage, but the principal repayment is forced saving, not spending.
- **Cash-flow break-even:** income covers every monthly cash outflow, so the bank balance stops falling.

Using the current ~£4,550/mo London net cash burn from [[london-monthly-budget|London Monthly Budget]], the ~£1,369/mo DB mortgage principal/equity component, and standard 2026/27 UK tax (net conversions approximate):

| Bar                                                              | Gross salary needed | Where it sits in the market                          | Difficulty for Julian                 |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| **Net-worth break-even floor** (covers consumption/non-equity costs; ex-principal ~£3,180/mo net) | ~£49-50k            | Well below median                                    | Very easy - over-qualified            |
| **Cash-flow break-even floor** (zero bank-balance drawdown; full ~£4,550/mo net cash burn) | ~£76-77k            | At/below the London architect **median** (£82.5-95k) | Easy - abundant roles, over-qualified |
| **Well-paid target**                                             | £150k+              | **Top decile (P90)**                                 | Hard - niche-lane-dependent           |

**The survival question and the well-paid question have opposite answers.** A cost-covering role sits *below* the market median where roles are abundant and Julian is over-qualified - a **high-probability** outcome. The report's own central case (£85-130k perm) already clears the cost-covering floor comfortably; it falls short only of the £150k *target*. All the difficulty is at the ceiling, not the floor.

**The real risk at the floor is not earning capacity - it is "over-qualified" screening** (a Global Head applying for a £75-90k role reads as a flight risk). That is a positioning/softening problem, far more manageable than the £150k stretch. And the runway backstops it: ~£4,550/mo is funded for about 4 years within Julian's red lines, so a 12-18 month search is covered 2-4x - the floor is protected twice, by high income-probability *and* deep runway.

**UK-vs-HK on the floor:** costs are roughly a wash-to-cheaper in London ([[uk-vs-hk-cost-comparison|Test A]]), so "easier to cover costs in the UK than HK" reduces to income-probability, where the UK's far larger role count helps even at the median. **UK-vs-HK income-probability was not independently researched** - HK is Julian's lived baseline. That head-to-head is the one comparison still open if he wants it evidenced rather than judged from experience.

## The crux: permanent vs contract, net of benefits

Julian's own decision rule is "compare **net of benefits**, never gross day-rate vs gross salary," because medical + life insurance are effectively **unobtainable independently post-cancer**. Applying that rule to the outside-IR35 contract routes (Ltd company, salary+dividends, 2026/27 rates):

| Contract rate (outside IR35) | Net cash @220 days | Verdict vs a £150k perm package |
|---|---|---|
| **£700/day** | ~£91k net | ≈ **identical net** to a £150k perm salary, but with **zero pension and zero medical/life cover**. **Strictly worse.** |
| **£775/day** | ~£99k net | +£8k cash, but foregone pension + insurance (~£11-14k+) **more than erases it**. Net break-even only at £775+/day **and** ~240 billable days. |
| Realistic year-1 (180 days) | £77-84k net | Well below perm. |

**Conclusion: outside-IR35 £700-775/day clears the headline turnover but NOT the £150k net-of-benefits floor.** Contract is worse than an equivalent permanent package for this candidate specifically, because he cannot buy back the benefits a perm employer provides. This confirms the profile's permanent tilt with numbers, and it demotes the contract route the raw day-rates made look easiest.

Inside-IR35 clarification: [[uk-150k-feasibility-jh-clarifications-2026-07-09]] estimates £775/day x 220 days at about £89.8k net if treated as an umbrella assignment rate, roughly cash-equivalent to a £150k permanent salary but without pension, medical, life cover, sick pay, income protection, and renewal-risk protection. Treat outside IR35 as upside, not the planning base case. See [[uk-ir35-contracting-feasibility-2026]].

**Why this matters:** the gut case for the move leaned on high UK contract rates. Those rates are real, but they do not survive the net-of-benefits test that Julian's own health situation forces. Permanent is the economically correct mode for him.

## Routes that clear £150k, re-ranked (contract demoted)

Ranked by realistic likelihood of clearing the £150k **net-of-benefits** floor:

1. **Permanent — Enterprise Architect / Head-of at the top of a London band.** Robert Half EA London P75 £153,750; Head of Enterprise Architecture P90 £164,681; Chief Architect £130-198k. **Moderate-hard** (needs a top-decile employer). **Best fit for the stated ideal:** perm + benefits + management, where the 90%+ team-leadership record carries the offer into the top band.
2. **Permanent — consultancy / Big-4 senior EA / principal.** Deloitte & KPMG EA P90 ~£151k. **Hard** (long funnels), but the best single answer to *both* the "never held the EA title" gap *and* the age question (consultancies value client-facing gravitas).
3. **Permanent — BFSI in-house security / architecture lead** (£130-160k). **Hard + gated** by the third-party assumption; where the "sector experience" framing is accepted, it is the cleanest perm path over £150k.
4. **Permanent — SI/vendor presales OTE ≥ £150k.** His **best skills fit** (presales revenue is his hook), but **thinnest UK data** at £150k — the clean £150k+ examples are US-inflated (Palo Alto, Cisco). Needs recruiter validation of real UK principal-presales OTE ceilings.
5. **Contract — Net-Sec / Solutions / IT Architect, outside IR35 (DEMOTED).** Clears gross but **not** net-of-benefits (see crux). Only economically viable at **£775+/day AND ~240 billable days**, and even then marginal. Use opportunistically for the scarce Net-Sec/SASE lane, not as the primary strategy.

**Routes that do not clear £150k for him:** single-domain **Cloud** (not positioned as a cloud specialist); any role bought at the **job-board median** (£82.5-95k); single-domain Network Architect contract (P50 £550 → £121k).

## Four load-bearing assumptions: final verdicts

| # | Assumption | Verdict | Detail |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | BFSI third-party experience satisfies "worked for a bank" | **MIXED, leans HOLDS for perm** | Most JDs ask for experience *within* financial services (domain/compliance), which his bank-embedded BT/AT&T/Telstra work satisfies; hard "worked *for* a Tier-1 bank" is the exception. **Shift:** HSBC/Barclays/Lloyds/Morgan Stanley are curbing PSC contractors post-IR35 → shrinks the BFSI *contract* premium, favours the *perm* route (where third-party reads fine). |
| 2 | UK is less age-discriminatory than HK (the profile's "core mover") | **Legal delta HOLDS; hiring-outcome delta UNPROVEN** | Verified: HK has **no** age-discrimination law (and no plan to legislate); UK has the Equality Act 2010. But legal recourse does **not** translate into a measured hiring-probability gain at 55, and UK tech ageism is endemic (61% of IT workers see prejudice; "too old" ~55). **Age is a secondary argument, not the core mover.** Anchor the move on market size + right-to-work. (The HK "43.1% over-50 participation" figure is real but 2019 and is a participation rate, not a discrimination measure.) |
| 3 | A cross-domain leader can win single-domain contracts at 55 | **MIXED** | Breadth reads as over-qualified/over-priced on commoditised single-domain contracts (no breadth premium). It wins only where scarcity is genuine (Net-Sec / SASE, where CCIE+CISSP+hands-on is rare). Fix: take breadth to **perm Head-of**; take contract only into the scarce lane. |
| 4 | HK location is a filter | **HOLDS while HK-based; MOOT once relocated** | An HK address is a binary pre-skills screen for many roles. Disappears with a UK (Wimbledon) address + right-to-work, except cleared/DV roles. Mitigation during transition: "relocating to London, available [date]," right-to-work confirmed. |

**Named hypothesis — "presales is mostly permanent; the higher-paying contract work is mostly in-house/delivery" — CONFIRMED.** Presales pay is base+commission, which only exists in employment (~90% perm). Contract volume and top day-rates concentrate in in-house delivery/transformation. He cannot get presales *and* a contract day-rate in one role; he picks the mode per opportunity — and per the crux, the mode should be perm.

## Market direction (the headwind nobody flagged first time)

No misread on either side, but the two honest series point in opposite directions and only one was foregrounded:
- **Demand up:** SA contract vacancies +87% YoY (909 → 1,706).
- **Price flat-to-down:** London EA advertised median £103.75k → £90k; security contract £675 → £635; SA day-rate flat despite the vacancy surge.

**Volume up, pay down is bearish for a pay-floor-premised move.** The original optimistic framing quoted the demand series and omitted the softening London price series. Net: don't assume rising demand lifts the pay ceiling; it isn't, in London, right now.

## Competition: does "more roles" survive the applicant test?

Supply (more UK roles) is only half the picture; this is the demand side. **Hard applicant-per-posting counts are not publicly retrievable** (LinkedIn's "X applicants" sits behind authentication; no board or recruiter publishes per-title counts), so the figures below are **estimates from labour-market tightness proxies, low confidence**, not observed counts.

- **Estimated applicants per senior-architect posting:** London/UK-hybrid ~50-150+ *raw* in the first 1-2 weeks, but only ~5-20 *genuinely qualified*, and for the rare-stack niche plausibly **low single digits**. Hong Kong ~30-100+ raw / ~5-15 qualified, with HK demand *cooling* in 2026 (net-negative employer headcount intent), pushing per-role competition up despite low unemployment.
- **Net verdict for this profile: the bigger UK market is *likely* a net edge, contingent on niche supply depth (Fable-reviewed).** The **supply-side half is verified**: the UK has ~5-18x more roles across his families (directionally sound; LinkedIn counts soft), and every tightness proxy points the right way — 2.14 jobseekers/vacancy and IT ~38 days-to-fill (Adzuna, May 2026); 37% of cyber vacancies hard-to-fill, ~143k workforce (DSIT 2025); niche day-rates £700-900+; HK demand net-negative (KPMG HK 2026). But those establish a *shortage in his lane*, not that his personal competitor pool is small.
- **The "net edge" claim rests on one unevidenced assumption:** that the rare-stack qualified *supply* (CCIE + CISSP + hands-on SASE + presales + leadership) stays scarce while niche demand scales with the role count. That supply side is **asserted, not evidenced.** A back-of-envelope national estimate spans **~14 to ~429 people (a 30x range)**, so any single figure is a midpoint of guesses and a low one flatters his scarcity — treat the pool size as **unknown, recruiter-to-confirm**. If the pool is deep, the edge shrinks toward a wash.
- **Two things the niche edge does NOT do:** it does not hold if he competes as a **generic SA / pure-cloud specialist** (that drops him into the oversupplied median glut where the bigger applicant pool genuinely bites); and it **removes one headwind, not all** — fewer niche competitors does not by itself clear £150k, nor clear the age screen.

Honest form: **compete in the niche, not the generalist median.** The shortage is concentrated in his lane, so the bigger UK market is *likely* a net edge — contingent on a niche supply depth a recruiter must confirm. Lane discipline makes the UK market-size advantage *possible*, not guaranteed. (Applicant estimates are low-confidence; per-posting counts are not public and the rare-stack pool size is unanchored.)

## What this means for the relocation (stated plainly)

This was commissioned to stress-test "easier to earn well in the UK than HK." The honest result:
- **Clearing £150k gross in the UK is a top-decile stretch for this profile, not a base case.** The expected outcome is £85-130k perm / £120-145k contract-equivalent, i.e. *below* the stated well-paid floor.
- **The best-fit £150k route is permanent** (Head-of / EA top-of-band, consultancy, or presales OTE), which also aligns with the benefits necessity. Contract, the gut preference, does not clear the net-of-benefits floor.
- **Age cannot be the reason to move.** The defensible pro-UK arguments are market size (far more architect roles than HK — but a real edge *only if he competes in his niche lane*; see Competition), right-to-work, and Julian's own read of the HK market. Age is a secondary, legal-not-outcome argument.

This does not by itself settle or reopen the relocation decision. It sharpens the income arm of the pre-agreed reopen condition. Under [[commitment-lock-protocol|the commitment protocol]], reopening is legitimate only if the new evidence satisfies the F-N-M-T test and the reopen condition: London is not materially cheaper and London job odds are not materially better. Current reading: the cost leg is met; the job-odds leg is still unproven, not yet met.

## Key Takeaways

- **Two different bars, opposite answers:** a job that *covers costs* (~£49-78k gross, depending on whether the equity/principal component is excluded) sits below the London architect median - abundant, and Julian is over-qualified, so **high-probability**. A *well-paid* £150k role is top-decile - hard. The survival floor is comfortably clearable; only the well-paid target is a stretch.
- **£150k+ gross is an upside, not the base case** — top-decile in every UK lane for this profile. Expected outcome is £85-130k perm / £120-145k contract-equivalent.
- **Contract does not beat a £150k perm package** once the (unbuyable, post-cancer) benefits gap is netted off. Permanent is the economically correct mode. This reverses the gut preference.
- **Best £150k routes are all permanent:** EA/Head-of top-of-band, consultancy senior EA, BFSI lead, or presales OTE (best skills-fit but thinnest data).
- **Age is a secondary argument for the move, not the core mover** — anchor on market size and right-to-work. (This contradicts [[target-role-profile]] Assumption 2 as currently worded; flagged for Julian to reconcile.)
- **Market is volume-up, London-pay-flat-to-down** — a mild headwind, not a tailwind.
- **The UK's bigger market is *likely* an edge, but only in the niche lane and only if the niche talent pool is genuinely scarce.** Hard applicant counts aren't public; the glut sits at the generalist median. "More roles beats more applicants" for him is a **contingent hypothesis, not a settled fact** — it needs a recruiter to confirm the rare-stack pool is actually small (plausible range spans 30x). And it removes one headwind, not all: it does not by itself clear £150k or the age screen.
- **The honest verdict on the original question:** a well-paid UK role is *attainable but hard*; whether it is *easier than HK* rests on market size (niche-lane only), Julian's own HK read, and lane discipline — not on income level or age.
