> *When you can't stop a manager taking credit, make the credit shared — and use that to your advantage.*

tags: [career]

## The Pattern

Some managers will take credit for your work. Fighting it directly is a losing battle — they hold the power, and calling it out makes you look difficult.

The smarter move: reframe the narrative so the credit is shared, not stolen.

## Tactics

- **Use "we" language proactively** — before the manager presents your work, position it as a joint effort in your own communications. This makes it harder for them to claim sole ownership without appearing ungrateful
- **Reinforce team narrative repeatedly** — consistently reference "what we've built together" in emails, updates, and meetings. Managers who like looking collaborative will adopt this framing
- **Make yourself visible at the source** — where possible, present directly to stakeholders, document your contributions in writing (emails, tickets, shared docs), and ensure your name is on artefacts
- **Don't try to outshine them** — pushing for personal recognition at their expense triggers defensiveness and political blowback
- **Build relationships skip-level** — visibility with their manager is a long-term hedge; your reputation is built over time, not in one moment

## What to Accept

A manager who takes credit is unlikely to start championing you individually. The realistic ceiling is that they present the team positively — which still reflects on you. That's the outcome to aim for.

If the environment consistently erodes your visibility and recognition, that's a signal — see [[career-strategy-and-office-politics]].

## My Pattern

Direct credit claims create political fights the manager will win. The "we" framing strategy requires being proactive before the moment arrives. Skip-level visibility is the longer-term hedge that changes the dynamic.

## Key Takeaways

- Direct credit claims are a losing fight; shared credit is achievable and often sufficient
- Use "we" framing early and consistently so the manager adopts it
- Build skip-level visibility and paper trails as a long-term hedge
- Persistent credit theft with no upside is a sign to move on
