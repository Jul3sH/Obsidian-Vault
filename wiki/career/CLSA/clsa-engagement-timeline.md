### Sep 2013 - Apr 2015 - BT/CLSA Principal Design Authority engagement

Julian was placed by British Telecom on-site at CLSA's Hong Kong office as **Principal Design Authority** - a de facto account CTO role. Rob was his primary CLSA stakeholder. The engagement finished on a high and the relationship has been maintained ever since.

#### Role scope
- **Architecture ownership:** Responsible for CLSA's end-to-end network architecture. Created the global standards for Nexus data centre fabrics (high-level design), with engineering teams completing the low-level design under Julian as design authority. Julian served as the escalation design authority for the programme.
- **ITSM:** Revised and documented CLSA's ITSM operational processes across service design, service operations, and service transition.
- **Full lifecycle:** Needs discovery through solution development to delivery.
- **Scope:** Infrastructure technology - networks, network security, unified communications, and data centre fabrics.

#### The dual data centre migration (standout achievement)
The defining piece of work. CLSA needed to upgrade equipment across two primary US data centres and migrate them to a new office - a highly complex and risky undertaking.

Julian's specific contribution was owning the **risk register for the entire project** - a role that emerged naturally because spotting and managing risk is a recognised strength. The migration was executed with exceptional methodology:

- **Result (US DCs):** 100% right-first-time success, 0% disruption to operations.
- **CIO quote:** Max Nam Strong (CLSA CIO at the time, whom Julian had met and got on well with) said afterwards: *"If I hadn't been told about it, I wouldn't have even known it was going on. It went so smoothly."*
- **Team recognition:** The whole CLSA technology team received accolades for the programme.

#### OTV - making Rob look good (relationship-defining moment)
A key piece of the migration de-risking was **OTV (Overlay Transport Virtualisation)** - a technology that prevents a failure in one data centre from cascading to and wiping out the second. Rob and Ed Sheklar (a key sponsor) were not initially keen to spend the money on it.

Julian walked Rob through the technology and its benefits in depth, which equipped Rob to make the case upward to Ed Sheklar and to CIO Max Nam Strong. They were persuaded and approved the investment. The outcome was visible at a senior level: **Ed Sheklar blogged about OTV internally to the company** - a public endorsement of the decision.

Julian effectively gave Rob the confidence and the ammunition to look good in front of his own sponsors. This is the relationship dynamic Rob would remember: Julian made him look good on the highest-stakes project of the engagement.

#### Service reviews and CIO face time
Julian regularly attended CLSA service reviews, which gave him direct access to Max Nam Strong (CIO) on multiple occasions. He used these sessions to build architecture pieces directly off the back of service review outcomes - turning operational feedback into design decisions. He was well received and well remembered across the CLSA technology organisation.

#### Geographic rollout
The architecture Julian created was not limited to the US. The Nexus DC fabric standards were also rolled out in Hong Kong while Julian was still on-site. After Julian left the engagement, the rollout continued to additional data centres. **The architecture is still in use at CLSA today.**

#### Departure
Julian moved on from CLSA in April 2015 - not due to any issue with the engagement, but because BT offered him multiple internal opportunities. He was promoted to Head of Design / Account CTO within BT shortly after.

#### Relationship outcome
Rob and Julian built strong mutual respect over the engagement. Rob experienced Julian at his best - methodical, technically rigorous, trusted with the highest-risk work, and delivering a landmark result. They have stayed in contact over the 10+ years since. Julian is not an unknown quantity at CLSA: Rob knows exactly what he gets.
