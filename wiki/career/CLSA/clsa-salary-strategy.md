## DECISION (locked, 16 June 2026)

**Respond to HR today with: last salary ~HK$155,800/month + bonus; expected ~HK$145,000/month + bonus; flexible.** Single clean number, framed as negotiable. (Decided after running the [[../../ai-os/skills/llm-council/SKILL|LLM Council]] on the anchor question.)

**Two hard constraints that shaped this:**
1. **NEVER mention Zhong Bing, Rob, or a "wider remit" to HR.** The Zhong conversation is informal and HR likely does not know about it. Referencing it could expose Rob for interfering with the HR process and **nullify the application**. The HR track is treated as a clean, standalone "Head of Network Services" application.
2. **Rob's 145 steer is off-record.** Rob said *"you didn't hear anything from me about salary expectation."* Do **not** message him to confirm the number or put it in writing. Julian owns the 145 figure himself - it is fully defensible as his own number, anchored on his last salary.

**Why 145 + bonus (not 150, not deferral):**
- 145 against a disclosed 155.8k last salary **signals a willing, realistic step-down** - which directly defuses HR's feared objection ("he'd never accept the drop, he'll be unhappy and leave"). A 150 ask reads as wanting to hold his old level; 145 reads as reasonable.
- Still **above HR's 115-125k band**, so he does not undersell.
- **Deferral was rejected:** the council's original "defer, cite scope" tactic is invalid here - it would require referencing Zhong (constraint 1). And Julian must respond today (HR emailed yesterday); silence/evasiveness risks annoying a cost-screener who already thinks he's expensive.
- **The wider-remit upside is NOT forfeited.** It lives on the separate, invisible Zhong track. If Zhong creates a bigger role, comp gets re-opened *there* - anchoring 145 with HR does not cap that.
- **"+ bonus"** keeps disclosure honest (a bonus existed) and apples-to-apples (CLSA also pays a bonus), without volunteering the scary ~2.08M total-comp figure. Give the 12.5% detail only if HR asks for full package.
- **Last salary rounded to ~155k (from verified 155,772.98).** Rounding *down* with "approximately" is conservative and honest (can only understate), and 155 → 145 reads as a clean, deliberate 10k step-down rather than an oddly precise figure.

### Considered and rejected: dropping to 140 (the "desperation" check)

Julian raised dropping the ask to **HK$140k** because he is anxious to secure work and **stay in Hong Kong**. Held at 145 after working it through. The reasoning, recorded so it isn't re-litigated:

- **140 buys no screen-out protection.** HR's ceiling is 125; both 145 and 140 sit *above* the band (140 is still ~12% over). Dropping 5k does **not** move him into their range or remove the "too expensive" friction - only ~125 or below would, which he should not give. So 140 is the worst of both worlds: it costs money *and* he's still "above band."
- **The 5k isn't the deciding factor.** What secures the role is Rob's advocacy + the Zhong wider-remit angle + fit. They decide whether they want him, *then* negotiate. Rob confirmed **they'll negotiate**, so they counter down from whatever he states - opening at 140 just lowers the ceiling (open 145 → land ~140; open 140 → land ~135).
- **You can always come down, never back up.** Keep 145 as the anchor; if it ever comes to a genuine "take ~138 or we walk" moment, concede *then*, from a position where they already want him. Pre-dropping discards that room.
- **Don't let desperation set the number.** The anxiety is about *getting the job*; 5k off the ask wouldn't change whether he gets it. Honour the desperation at the closing table if forced, not in the opening line.
- **145 is the one piece of inside intel.** Rob effectively pegged it ("slightly high, but they'll negotiate"). Second-guessing it down to 140 discards that signal.

**Conclusion:** send at 145. If money turns out to be the only gap, that's a real signal - flex then, eyes open, not pre-emptively.

## The objectives (the sweet spot)

The number Julian states must thread two competing failure modes:

1. **Don't come in so high that HR rules him out.** HR already flagged the CV as "too expensive". An asking number too far above their band risks being screened out before Zhong Bing ever sees him. HR's posture is that they *"will look to negotiate"* - so the asking number needs to be negotiable down to something acceptable to them, not an instant disqualifier.

2. **Don't come in so low that he undersells himself.** If the Zhong Bing conversation lands (see below), Zhong becomes a sponsor motivated to *find extra money* for a high-value hire. Anchoring too low forfeits that upside and leaves money on the table.

## The lever that changes the maths - the Zhong Bing wider-remit play

Rob's strategy is **not** to win the salary on the base Head of Network Services role. It is to get Julian **in front of Zhong Bing** (Deputy CIO, now owns Tech Operations), who is hunting for good people.

- The pitch to Zhong is a **wider remit than the base role**: Julian also taking on the **operational/stability side** - exactly Zhong's fixation after the undetected port-speed fault.
- Julian's **ITIL Expert + system/service-design** background is the credential that makes the operational narrative credible.
- **If Zhong sees the value, the budget conversation changes:** a hire scoped to network *plus* operational uplift justifies more than HR's base-role band. Zhong, not HR, becomes the one finding the money.

**Implication for the number:** the asking figure should leave room for this upside. Coming in at HR's band concedes the wider-remit value before it has been argued.
