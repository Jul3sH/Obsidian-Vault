# UK £150k Feasibility - JH Clarifications

## Key Takeaways

- The report was too compressed on contract versus permanent. The decisive point is not just cash: it is employer pension, group medical, death-in-service, income protection, and the fact that some individual insurance may be unavailable or heavily excluded after cancer.
- Inside IR35 weakens the contract route further. At an umbrella assignment rate of £775/day for 220 days, estimated net is about £89.8k, roughly the same cash as a £150k permanent salary after tax, but without the permanent benefits.
- Age 55 is a real hiring risk in the UK. The law protects against age discrimination, but UK hiring outcomes are not age-neutral. Treat age as a headwind to mitigate, not a reason to assume the UK is safe.
- The correct relocation logic is not "the decision is committed, so ignore new evidence." The correct logic is: new evidence can reopen only if it meets the [[commitment-lock-protocol|F-N-M-T reopen test]] and the pre-agreed reopen condition.
- The remaining hard gaps are live-market gaps: principal presales OTE, applicant counts, West Corridor volume, and BFSI wording in current JDs. Desk research cannot honestly close those.

## 1. Can over-qualified screening be softened?

Partly, not completely.

It is realistic to soften a "Global Head applying for a £75-90k job" problem by framing the move as intentional:

- "Relocating to London for family and schooling."
- "Looking for a hands-on architecture leadership role, not a global corporate track."
- "Happy to be close to delivery and presales again."
- "Willing to trade global scope for the right London-based platform."

That softening is more credible in the UK than in Hong Kong if the target role genuinely uses seniority, client credibility, and leadership. It is less credible for a median generic architect role where the hiring manager sees obvious flight risk.

The defensible answer is: over-qualified screening is manageable at the cost-covering floor, but it is still the real floor risk. It should not be dismissed just because the UK is less status-rigid than Asia.

## 2. What research was done on UK versus HK income probability?

The UK feasibility report researched the UK side in depth. The later [[uk-vs-hk-earning-comparison]] researched the HK comparison, tax difference, salary bands, and broad market direction.

What was not researched to the same standard is a live, matched probability model:

| Question | Current state |
|---|---|
| UK role volume by target cell | Directionally researched, not live-filtered with login data |
| HK role volume by target cell | Directionally researched, thinner than UK |
| Applicant counts per matching role | Not publicly available without authenticated job-site access |
| Julian's interview probability by cell | Not directly measurable from desk research |
| £150k UK versus HK landing probability | Reasoned judgment, not a measured probability |

So Julian was right to challenge this. The honest wording is not "the comparison is done." It is: the UK floor looks easier because the floor salary is low and role volume is larger, but the well-paid UK versus HK probability remains a live validation task.

## 3. Why is contract worse than an equivalent permanent package?

There are two separate arguments.

### Cash argument

Using 2026/27 UK tax and NI rates, and treating inside-IR35 rates as umbrella assignment rates:

| Route | Assumption | Estimated net cash |
|---|---:|---:|
| Permanent | £150k salary | ~£91.3k/yr, ~£7.6k/mo |
| Inside IR35 | £700/day, 220 days | ~£82.2k/yr, ~£6.9k/mo |
| Inside IR35 | £775/day, 220 days | ~£89.8k/yr, ~£7.5k/mo |
| Inside IR35 | £775/day, 240 days | ~£96.9k/yr, ~£8.1k/mo |
| Inside IR35 | £850/day, 220 days | ~£97.3k/yr, ~£8.1k/mo |

At normal utilisation, £775/day inside IR35 is not a clear cash win over £150k permanent. At 240 billable days it becomes a cash win, but 240 days is high utilisation and still excludes the permanent benefits stack.

### Benefits argument

Permanent can include benefits that a contractor must replace alone:

| Benefit | Conservative economic reading |
|---|---|
| Employer pension | Legal minimum can be small, but senior roles often matter only if the employer pays on full salary. A 5 percent employer pension on £150k is £7.5k/yr. |
| Private medical insurance | Group cover is valuable because access may be easier than individual underwriting after cancer. The value is not just the average premium. |
| Death-in-service life cover | Typical senior packages can be 4x salary. On £150k, that is a possible £600k contingent benefit for Sophia. Individual replacement may be expensive, excluded, or unavailable. |
| Income protection / sick pay | Often underweighted in contractor comparisons. Cancer history makes this more important, not less. |
| Paid leave and bench risk | A 220-day contract year already removes holiday from revenue. It does not remove renewal, sickness, and gap risk. |

The contract route must beat both cash and the benefits stack. For Julian specifically, the "unbuyable or excluded" part matters as much as the premium-equivalent part.

## 4. Elaborating the "why this matters" line

The old instinct was: UK contract day rates are high, so the move can be economically justified by contract upside.

The corrected logic is harsher:

- The headline turnover can look like £150k.
- Inside IR35 strips much of the advantage.
- Outside IR35 is better but cannot be assumed.
- The benefits gap is unusually important because of post-cancer underwriting and Sophia dependency.
- Therefore contract is not the clean route to the "well-paid" target.

The move can still be justified, but not by pretending high contract turnover equals a permanent £150k package.

## 6. What is the realistic chance of a 55-year-old landing a permanent job?

Not safe enough to hand-wave. Plausible, but definitely not a base-rate free pass.

The positive case:

- Senior architecture, presales, consulting, and Head-of roles can value gravitas, client confidence, and leadership.
- Julian's recent title, applied AI work, CCIE/CISSP/TOGAF stack, and live relocation to London all help.
- Permanent may fit better than contract because it lets him sell leadership, stability, and stakeholder management, not just narrow technical delivery.

The negative case:

- UK age discrimination is illegal, but that does not make hiring outcomes equal.
- Totaljobs research reported by The Times and The Guardian found material recruiter age bias, including 46 percent of recruiters viewing 57 as too old.
- A 55-year-old with a global-head title applying below senior leadership can be read as expensive, temporary, or hard to manage.

Verdict: he can land permanent, but it must be targeted. Best cells are senior-presales, consultancy, Head-of, BFSI architecture lead, and niche SASE/network-security leadership. Generic median architect roles are where age plus over-qualified screening bite hardest.

## 7. Why was the report talking about outside IR35?

Because outside IR35 is the only contract form that can make the day-rate route look competitive with a £150k permanent package.

That was too narrow. The report should also show inside IR35, because many large clients, especially banks and large enterprises, are cautious after off-payroll reforms.

Short answer:

- Outside IR35: can produce better cash, but status depends on the real working arrangement and the client decision.
- Inside IR35: taxed broadly like employment, with employer NI and umbrella costs often reducing the contractor's effective taxable pay.
- For Julian: inside IR35 at £700-775/day does not beat a £150k perm package once benefits are counted.

Separate note: [[uk-ir35-contracting-feasibility-2026]].

## 8. "Volume up, pay down" in plain English

Yes: the report means demand can rise while pay does not rise.

That can happen if:

- More vacancies are mid-band roles, not top-band roles.
- Employers are backfilling churn rather than bidding up scarce talent.
- The market has more openings but also more candidates.
- Budget pressure pushes hiring volume into cheaper bands.

So "more vacancies" is good for landing *a* job. It is not automatically good evidence for landing a £150k job.

## 10. "But will they hire a 55-year-old?"

This is the right sceptical question.

The answer is not "yes." The answer is: only in roles where age reads as seniority, not obsolescence.

Better targets:

- Principal presales / consulting architecture, where client credibility matters.
- Head-of architecture / network-security / infrastructure, where management experience matters.
- BFSI or regulated-sector architecture lead, where judgment and risk experience matter.
- Niche SASE / network-security architecture, where the CCIE plus CISSP stack is a differentiator.

Worse targets:

- Generic cloud architect.
- Median solution architect role with no leadership scope.
- Contract roles that want a narrow implementer under a younger programme lead.
- Roles where the hiring manager sees the global-head history as a threat or a short-term bridge.

The CV and LinkedIn should therefore lead with currency: recent applied AI work, current architecture problems, SASE/security relevance, and hands-on leadership.

## 11. What evidence supports "age cannot be the reason to move"?

The evidence supports only a narrow claim:

- UK law protects age as a characteristic and restricts discriminatory recruitment practices.
- Hong Kong lacks equivalent age-discrimination legislation.

That is a legal-system delta. It is not proof that a 55-year-old has better hiring outcomes in the UK.

The UK evidence cuts both ways: legal protection exists, but age bias still appears in recruiter surveys and older-worker labour-market commentary. Therefore age can be a supporting argument, not the core mover.

The stronger pro-UK evidence is:

- larger relevant role market,
- right to work and local address after relocation,
- easier cost-covering floor,
- better fit with Sophia and family strategy,
- runway.

## 12. Does this settle or reopen the relocation decision?

The report should not imply that prior commitment automatically settles the issue.

Better wording:

> This report does not by itself settle or reopen the relocation decision. It sharpens Test B. Under the commitment protocol, reopening is legitimate only if the new evidence satisfies the F-N-M-T test and the pre-agreed reopen condition: London is not materially cheaper and London job odds are not materially better.

Current reading:

- Test A cost leg: met. London is not materially cheaper.
- Test B job-odds leg: not fully met from current evidence. The £150k target is hard, but the cost-covering floor still looks easier in the UK and the UK market-size edge remains plausible in the niche lane.

So this clarification weakens any "UK £150k is easy" story. It does not yet prove "London job odds are not materially better." The missing work is live validation.
