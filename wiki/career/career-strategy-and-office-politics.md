# Career Strategy and Office Politics

## Back the Right Horse

- Becoming indispensable to a weak or unpopular manager is a trap — when that manager is replaced, incoming leadership brings their own trusted people
- People surround themselves with those they already trust; external credibility doesn't transfer automatically
- Before investing heavily in a relationship upward, assess whether that person has genuine organisational standing — if their opinion isn't respected, yours won't be either by proxy
- See also: [[working-with-others/internal-networking-and-relationship-building]]

## Always Have Options — Walk Before Being Pushed

- Proactive job searching from a position of employment is far more powerful than reactive searching after leaving
  - You are more confident, less desperate, and can afford to be selective
  - Interview preparation is lighter and less stressful when the role is aspirational rather than urgent
- Waiting for the writing on the wall to become unavoidable is a form of laziness — act on early signals
- The default bias is to rationalise staying ("I'll have no problem getting something") even when the evidence points to an exit
- See also: [[working-with-yourself/beating-procrastination-face-fear-get-started]]

## Know What You Actually Want — Work Hard, Play Hard

- Achievement is the core driver — the satisfaction after a gruelling 16-hour problem-solving session, not just holding a title
- Leadership is a means to achieve better outcomes and get respect for results — not an end in itself
- The distinction between "achievement-motivated" and "leadership-motivated" is important for career targeting: chase roles that reward delivery, not just seniority
- Pressure and intensity are acceptable; what matters is the payoff feeling on the other side
- Ref: chapters 4 and 5 of *Getting Unstuck* on alpha leader vs achiever

## Know Your Career Type — Not Just Technical

- Broad, commercially-oriented roles (consultancy, presales, governance, financial risk) are a better fit than deep technical execution roles
- Enjoyed: financial discussions, governance and costing tools, commercial risk, working across disciplines, personality-led client engagement
- Didn't enjoy: hardcore technical delivery, technical presales, pure architect or technical analyst roles
- Don't over-index on one bad experience — a technical consultancy role doesn't rule out consultancy in general; the context matters
- See also: [[working-with-others/kroll-case-study]]

## Define and Communicate Your Brand

- State your unique combination of skills clearly so others can grasp and repeat it — this is critical for your CV and word-of-mouth reputation
- Being respected for your achievements and opinions is a deep motivator — make sure your brand reflects this explicitly
- Must be clear on: purpose, aims, goals — and bake recognition into them honestly
- See also: [[working-with-others/communication-style-and-personal-brand]]

## Quit While You're Ahead

- Know when to stop talking — when a conversation is going well, adding more questions or problems can undermine the goodwill you've built
- When someone senior is stretched or under pressure, adding to their load poisons the interaction regardless of your intent
- The lesson: read the room, finish on a high note, and resist the urge to squeeze more out of a positive moment

## How Promotions Actually Happen — Gio's Case Study

Gio's promotion was not primarily about technical skill. The real drivers:

- Generated measurable revenue for the business
- Built relationships with key internal decision-makers over time
- Rode the success of a respected senior sponsor (Stuart) and secured his active support
- Had team leadership on his CV — visible signal of seniority
- Understood the corporate culture and way of operating
- Was a familiar name to senior stakeholders from prior exposure (Architecture team / Christine)
- Took credit proactively through self-promotion to people who lacked context to verify it
- Was opportunistic — stepped into a gap (LSA deployment without a support model) at the right moment

**Conclusion:** promotion is less about merit alone and more about cultivating the right relationship with the right person at the right time.

- See also: [[working-with-others/internal-networking-and-relationship-building]], [[working-with-others/managing-up-bring-solutions-communicate-success]]

## Choose Wisely When to Introduce Change

- Never change your systems, processes, or methods when you are also adapting to a new environment
- Starting a new role is already cognitively demanding — adding a new task management system on top creates overload, not improvement
- Wait until you have the environment under control before introducing personal process changes
- This principle extends beyond task management: any major methodology shift should wait for stable ground
- See also: [[working-with-yourself/prioritisation-and-scheduling]]

## My Pattern

Staying too long before acting is a documented recurring pattern. Rationalisation masks the discomfort of being on the market. The "quit while ahead" principle is essential: conversations that go well can be damaged by talking past the winning moment.

## Key Takeaways

- Back managers who have genuine standing — becoming indispensable to the wrong person wastes political capital
- Always be proactively searching; options give you confidence and leverage
- Know your motivation type: achievement-driven is different from leadership-driven, and should shape your role choices
- Target commercially and interpersonally broad roles over deep technical execution
- Define your brand explicitly and ensure others can articulate it on your behalf
- Promotions hinge on sponsorship, visibility, timing, and perceived value — not just performance
- Quit while ahead: learn to end interactions on a high rather than talking past the winning moment
- Never change systems or methods when settling into a new environment — stabilise first
