# Objective Interview Preparation

## The Core Failure

Subjective preparation means spending time on content that is interesting or comfortable rather than on what the role actually demands. It feels like diligent preparation but produces the wrong output.

Classic form: deep technical prep for a leadership/service-design role — the familiar content that feels safer to study — while leaving the role-specific requirements, company knowledge, and non-technical questions underprepared.

## Understand the Role Before Preparing

- Ask explicitly what the role entails before investing preparation time — never assume
- Find out what "hitting the floor running" means for this specific position
- Ask what success looks like in the first 90 days; validate you can meet that bar
- If you have gaps — name them directly and explain how you'd bridge them; skirting around gaps reads worse than owning them

## Prioritise Objectively

- List what the role actually requires, then prepare in that priority order
- Textbook answers, company knowledge, and role clarity beat deep technical prep for senior roles
- Prepare non-technical content: interests outside work, personal values, why this company, clever questions to ask
- Prepare hooks — answers steered toward your strengths that go slightly beyond the question

## Validate Requirements — Never Assume

- Interviewers rarely volunteer the full picture; extract it through preparation research and early-stage conversations
- Get specific: what does the team need most right now? What gaps are they hiring to fill?
- Assumptions left unvalidated become gaps that surface at the worst moment

## Prepare to Sell Yourself at the Edges

- Have strong answers for: interests outside work, values, why this company over others
- These questions probe authenticity and cultural fit — interviewers remember weak answers here
- Have questions prepared that signal you've understood the role deeply and are already thinking about how to succeed in it

## Key Takeaways

- Subjective interest is the enemy of objective preparation — always prepare for the role, not the interview
- Understand the role inside out before the final round; never assume
- Prepare the edges: non-technical questions, interests, values, and clever questions to ask
- Own gaps directly — a confident plan beats a gap you try to hide

## My Pattern

Preparation invested in technically interesting content at the expense of role-relevant content is the direct pattern. The pull toward the familiar feels like preparation but misses the mark. Non-technical questions are consistently underprepared because they feel less important — but they are often decisive.
