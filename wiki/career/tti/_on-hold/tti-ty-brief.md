AI generates the allocation rules and the bill-of-IT fast; Julian brokers the consensus. It compresses as allocation data standardises (the FOCUS-spec risk), so this is a window to exploit, not a franchise to rest on. (Verbal only - never put the window's expiry in writing to Ty.)
- **Status signal for the room:** saying cleanly *"FinOps is the cloud corner and it's commoditising; TBM is the whole estate, and that's the layer that isn't"* marks Julian as operating above the cloud-cost-tool level.

**The one line that unifies all three pillars:** *"AI automates the preparation for judgment, not the accountability for judgment."* Said three ways (cost = allocation consensus, security = risk acceptance, EA = governance accountability), it makes Julian sound like one coherent method, not three pitches.

### C1. Resource-design mechanics (fills rows 3 and 5)

**The toolbox - pick the smallest effective tool for the bottleneck (never force everything into one AI pattern):**

| Bottleneck type | Best-fit approach |
|---|---|
| Repetitive UI actions (desktop / browser) | task mining, then RPA / lightweight automation |
| End-to-end cross-team delays | process mining + BPMN redesign |
| Stable system actions with back-end APIs | API workflow / BPMN service tasks |
| Judgment-heavy work (triage, summarise, classify) | agent loop / bounded AI task |
| High-risk actions needing traceability | BPMN orchestration + rules + human approval |

**The liability taxonomy - this IS "resource design," the line itself:**
- **A. AI-autonomous** - pattern tasks AI may assert without a human.
- **B. AI-informs, human-approves** - AI prepares the answer; a human decides.
- **C. Human-owned accountability** - AI cannot touch (risk acceptance, disclosure, privilege changes).

BPMN keeps AI inside a governed, visible process, not a black box - the operational twin of the security stop-points (row 5) and the EA accountability line. **Same method, three vocabularies**, which is what makes Julian sound like one coherent operator, not three pitches.

> **Framing guardrail:** "observe the work" is *"watch how the work really gets done, with the team, in the open."* Never *"deploy task-mining software on employee desktops"* - that has privacy / works-council implications a CFO's HR and legal colleagues will hear instantly.

### C2. The 90-day timebox (fills row 6)

The value-prop's four stages, mapped to one timebox:
1. **Observe the work (weeks ~1-4):** watch how the work really gets done, with the team, in the open.
2. **Find the bottleneck:** a sized pipeline ranked by effort, impact and ease of adoption. **The stop decision (kill-clause) sits here**, at the discovery/build boundary.
3. **Apply the right tool (weeks ~5-12):** the smallest effective intervention on one workflow, taken to a working, measured proof.
4. **Measure and expand:** prove the win; the recurring saving lands from here. Expansion is the verbal answer to "and if it works?", never offered in writing.

**Offer Ty: one timebox, one workflow, one decision gate at week 4-6, one kill-clause.** No tiers, no menu. The four-tier ladder (Discovery Sprint, Quick Win Build, Workflow Redesign, Continuous Efficiency Program) stays here as Julian's internal scoping model; a "Continuous Program" in the brief would undercut "simple to start and simple to stop."
