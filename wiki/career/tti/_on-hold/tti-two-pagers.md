# TTI — The Four Two-Pagers

One short, forwardable, per-executive report. Unifying spine: **process re-engineering → AI-enabled workflows → selectively, agents.** Each tailored to the recipient's mandate, each closing on a small concrete ask (a 30-minute conversation about one candidate workflow).

## Design decisions baked in
- **Conditional framing** — none of these assert TTI has a problem. Each says, in effect, *"I'm not assuming this is you; but where it is, here's the value."* (Avoids presuming knowledge of an exec's internal state and getting their back up.)
- **ISO/IEC 42001 — placed proportionally, not uniformly** (dropping it in all four reads as consultant-bingo):
  - **Ortiz (GRC): central** — a real beat; strongest differentiator (governance, not data science).
  - **Ty (EBIT): one light line** — tied to margin durability only.
  - **Uva (CIO): optional, light** — reference-architecture framing.
  - **Zimmermann (PM/CI): omitted** — delivery throughput isn't a governance-standard story.
  - Register is always *"aligned to / informed by,"* never "required" — 42001 is the emerging AI-management-system standard, not yet a legal requirement.
- **ONE-KEY two-pager dropped** from the set (product/platform-ecosystem play, not internal process optimisation — different track).

## Two-pager #1 — Ty Staviski

**AI, Applied to the G&A Cost Line**
*How process optimisation converts TTI's AI adoption into EBIT margin*
**Prepared for:** Ty Staviski, Group Deputy CFO · **From:** Julian Hart · **June 2026**

**1. Turning AI adoption into margin — the pattern, and the question it raises**

The brief I shared earlier this year opened with a finding worth restating: across a 2025 survey of ~6,000 executives, **80%+ of enterprises report no measurable AI productivity gain** despite widespread adoption. The cause is rarely the technology. Left ungoverned, people use AI to reduce their own effort rather than to change cycle times, throughput, or headcount — so the organisation captures the activity but not the margin. MIT-linked research finds the same: generic AI works at the individual level and stalls at the enterprise level unless it's integrated into workflows and calibrated to company objectives.

I'm not assuming that's TTI's position — **you'll know far better than I would** how much of the AR25 intent (*"leverage AI and synergies to drive EBIT margin improvement,"* taking out structural G&A cost toward 10% EBIT by 2027) has already converted to the P&L. The point is narrower: *to the extent any of it is still in flight*, the thing that closes the gap is an ownership layer over the translation — process re-engineering, governance, and the cost discipline to hold the gain. **Where TTI already has that layer, this isn't the conversation. Where it doesn't — that's exactly the work I do.**

**2. Where it converts in your world**

Four G&A process areas where AI-enabled workflow redesign maps to a cost line, not a productivity anecdote:

- **Financial close & management reporting** — reconciliation, variance commentary, consolidation prep, redesigned around AI with human judgement reserved for exceptions: shorter close, fewer hands, lower run-cost.
- **Procurement & accounts payable** — invoice handling, PO matching, contract and spend analysis; AI-native exception handling compresses cycle time and surfaces leakage.
- **FP&A & board reporting** — first-draft analysis, scenario prep, recurring reports — a smaller analyst load covering more of the business.
- **IT & vendor cost (TBM)** — mapping technology spend to business service and EBIT contribution, then governing it down. My home discipline.

The point is not "give finance staff Copilot." It is **role-class redesign** — what each function looks like when AI is the default operating mode — with the gain *re-baselined into the budget* so it shows up as cost-out, not slack.

**3. Why this is a process-architecture problem, not a data-science one**

Closing this gap needs someone who understands corporate operations at a systems level, can design the governance and evaluation framework, and can re-baseline KPIs and hold the organisation to them. That is enterprise architecture and service design with financial discipline attached — not model-building. It is complementary to the AI platform team: the platform makes the work possible; this layer makes it pay.

**4. What I bring to this specifically**

- **CTBME and FinOps** practitioner — I map IT and operational spend to measurable EBIT contribution in the same TBM language as your mandate.
- **Cost track record:** restructured a design function to **35% sustainable saving in a single year** and **6%+ YoY** efficiencies; assured **US$200M+** of complex technology designs on cost, performance, and commercial viability at BT.
- **30+ years delivering under US technical leadership across APAC** — the bridge between the Milwaukee agenda and the regional operating reality where much of the G&A footprint sits.

**5. The ask**

Not a role — a **30-minute conversation about one candidate workflow.** Pick the G&A line where the cost is most visible; I'll come with a 90-day pilot scoped to a measurable target — headcount/cost baseline, productivity uplift drawn from published enterprise benchmarks, and a defensible ROI methodology that holds up to finance scrutiny. And margin from AI only compounds if the automation behind it is governed — designing to a reference like **ISO/IEC 42001** (the emerging AI management-system standard) protects the gain from later board, audit, or regulatory remediation cost. The full NPV model exists; the pilot proves it on one real process first.

*The pilot can run as a 90-day engagement or as the first deliverable of a role — a question for the conversation, not this note.*

*How process optimisation scales EA governance and platform standardisation across regions*
**Prepared for:** Tom Uva, CIO · **From:** Julian Hart · **June 2026**

**1. Turning AI adoption into a governed, scalable platform**

Across enterprises, 80%+ report no measurable AI productivity gain despite broad adoption — a governance problem, not a technology one. The same pattern *can* show up inside the architecture function itself: AI used ad hoc by individuals, while the things that actually scale a platform — consistent standards, fast design review, current documentation, transparent technology cost — stay manual and uneven across regions. **I won't presume that's TTI's position; you'll know where your function already has this in hand.** The point is narrower: *where any of it is still manual*, that's where AI-enabled governance converts into faster, more consistent delivery — and TTI's drive to **standardise platforms and scale services across diverse manufacturing geographies** is exactly the work that benefits.

**2. Where it bites in your world**

Four EA-governance processes where AI-enabled workflows convert into faster, more consistent delivery:

- **Design & solution review** — AI-assisted first-pass review against standards and reference architectures, so human review time is spent on judgement, not completeness-checking.
- **Architecture Decision Records & documentation** — auto-drafted ADRs and living documentation generated from design artefacts, keeping the EA repository current instead of perpetually stale.

This is **role-class redesign for the architecture practice** — what governance looks like when AI is the default operating mode — not a Copilot licence bolted onto existing review queues.

**3. Why this is a process-architecture problem, not a data-science one**

Automating EA governance well requires someone who knows the discipline from the inside — TOGAF practice, governance frameworks, service design — and can re-engineer it around AI. That is enterprise architecture augmented with AI delivery, complementary to the data/AI platform team rather than competing with it: the platform supplies the capability; this makes the architecture function itself scale.

**4. What I bring to this specifically**

- **TOGAF-certified, hands-on EA governance** across FTSE100 and global MNC environments; governed architecture across **US$200M+** of solutions to cost, capability, and quality.
- **100+ design standards and architecture blueprints**, with reusable patterns that **cut design cycle times by 20%+** — the exact lever this proposal scales with AI.
- **Full-lifecycle range** — strategy and roadmapping, architecture design, governance and assurance, service design, continuous improvement (ITIL Expert) — and 30+ years bridging US technical leadership with APAC delivery.

**5. The ask**

Not a role — a **30-minute conversation about one candidate area** (design review or ADR generation are the cleanest starting points). I'll bring a 90-day pilot scoped to a measurable target: review-cycle time, documentation currency, or standards conformance, with a defensible before/after methodology. The full framework exists; the pilot proves it on one real governance process first.

80%+ of enterprises capture no measurable AI gain from adoption — a governance problem, not a tooling one. In security, the detection layer is typically already well-served: I'm assuming a mature SOC with a SIEM and established automation. The layer with the most room is usually **GRC** — governance, risk, and compliance — where, across most enterprises, the work stays manual and evidence-heavy even where the SOC is highly automated. **I won't presume that's your position; you'll know where your GRC function already runs lean.** Where it doesn't, AI-enabled process redesign converts effort into a measurably stronger, faster, lower-cost risk posture — and TTI's globally distributed manufacturing and supply-chain footprint, with its OT/IT convergence and regulatory load, is exactly the environment where that compounds.

**2. Where it bites in your world**

Four GRC processes where AI-enabled workflows convert directly to coverage and cycle time — without touching the SOC:
