## ⭐ The single most important thing

**Convert the ask from "believe in me" to "approve the audit and set the threshold yourself."** Nothing is survivable if Ty senses he's being asked to spend political capital on faith in a candidate with an unproven track record during a CFO transition. The audit-first reframe neutralises, in one move, all four risks at once: the prior-proof gap (case no longer rests on Julian's history), the Frank vacuum (nothing needs chartering yet), the Tony risk (nothing reaches Tony yet), and the stall read (it produces the number faster than any alternative). **Architect the meeting so its last five minutes are Ty specifying what he wants on that one page.** Leave with that = a sponsor in motion. Leave with warm interest and no Ty-owned action = the 15 July call again, a month later and a month closer to the deadline.

## ⚠ Readiness verdict: ~80%. Do NOT delay the meeting, but it's 1-2 days of prep from ready, not zero.

**Priority order before the Ty meeting:**
1. **Build the prior-proof story (genuine blocker).** The honest bridge survives a receptive Ty once; it will NOT survive Ty retelling it to Tom Uva. ~One working session: reconstruct one concrete Telstra International before/after (a named reconciliation forum / approval loop / recurring escalation that the design authority + waiver process made unnecessary — what it was, what replaced it, what stopped), plus the same for the US$300M transformation. ## 1. Simulated transcript — the August Ty meeting (HK, in person)

**TY:** Julian, good to do this properly. Last time was a handshake call; this is the real one. Let me replay where I left it. We have technology leadership in Asia that's fragmented — capable people, but siloed — and the way the organisation copes is steering committees. Lots of them. I sit on some. Nothing crosses a region without one, and half decide nothing. I said we need an enterprise architect working with the US to put frameworks and governance in place. You've had three weeks. What does the fix actually look like?

**JULIAN:** You've got it right, and I'd add one thing before the fix: the fragmentation isn't a disease, it's the rational residue of how TTI grew. You bought Milwaukee, Hoover, Vax; each integration call was sensible in isolation, and decentralisation is part of what got you to fourteen billion. So I'm not proposing to cure the estate. The committees aren't the problem, they're the symptom. What's missing is only the layer that stops the next cross-region decision needing a committee. Concretely: SAP group-wide, Oracle Cloud in NA finance, Oracle EBS legacy in HK — three ERP tiers, no group reference architecture governing how they align. The committees are how the org reconciles across that gap, meeting by meeting.

**TY:** Fair diagnosis, and finance meets it at every close as reconciliation effort, so you're preaching to the choir. But my problem with the word "governance": everyone who walks in here says it, and it always means one more committee with a new name and a new chair. What structurally replaces the committees?

**JULIAN:** A standing Architecture Board with chartered decision rights, on a small set of agreed principles — conform to the group reference architecture unless a waiver is granted, reuse before buy before build, conform to group integration standards unless waived. The principles stop every decision becoming a fresh committee fight. And the sequencing matters: the board and principles deliver value in week one, because they resolve live decisions immediately. You don't need the finished target architecture to stop the next divergence. Decisions first, documents second. Committees retire as decisions get a home, not years later when a document lands.

**TY:** Okay. Hard version. I chair three of those steering committees today. Every region's in the room, everyone's polite, nobody agrees, item rolls to next month. Your board has the same people. Why does yours converge when mine don't?

**JULIAN:** Two things written into the charter that your committees don't have. First, a decision SLA: the board resolves an item within a set window or it auto-escalates to the charter executive. It can't roll indefinitely; the calendar forces it. Second, a ratification rule: I chair and recommend, the named executive ratifies, silence is consent not a park. Without those, you're right — pooled authority just means everyone holds a veto and the room stalls politely. "Forces convergence" has to be a written mechanism, not a verb in my mouth. If that charter isn't real, I wouldn't take the role, because then it's just another committee.

**TY:** Good answer. Now break it. Auto-escalates to who? Frank's out the door — you know that. I'm the Deputy, and I'll be straight: I don't own the technology line. Tom Uva does. And a chunk of this touches Milwaukee, where IT doesn't report to anyone in Asia. Who's the name at the top of your escalation path?

**JULIAN:** The load-bearing question, and I'll give you the design requirement honestly rather than pretend I can read your org chart from outside. Two things must be true. The charter has to be joint: the business sponsor who owns the cost of the chaos — you, you raised it — paired with group technology leadership at Tom Uva's tier. A finance-only charter over technology directors reads as architecture by spreadsheet, and it dies. And Frank can't be load-bearing; a charter resting on him dies at his farewell. Then the escalation terminus: one desk whose authority sits above both the APAC technology directors and Milwaukee. From outside, that looks like the CEO or a jointly constructed authority between you and Tom. You know which it actually is. That's a decision I'd make with you, not sell to you.

**TY:** Honest answer: above both lines is the CEO. And I am not walking into the CEO's office to ask for an escalation charter for a role that doesn't exist, for a candidate he's never met, in the quarter our Group CFO is leaving. That chip doesn't exist. So what's the version I can actually stand up?

**JULIAN:** The version where the terminus is only reached when a deadlock crosses both lines. Day to day the ratifier is Tom for anything inside the technology line, you as joint sponsor carrying the cost case. The CEO-level terminus is a backstop you design in but expect to use maybe once a year — and by then the board has a track record and a number, so the conversation is "settle this one item," not "bless a new structure." You charter what you and Tom can charter now; the backstop gets formalised once it's proven it converges.

**TY:** More workable. I'll flag it's also more fragile — a backstop you add later is one Tony's people know isn't there yet. Park it; back to Tony shortly. First, the question I'd ask any director-level hire, the specific version not the resume version. Where have you run exactly this? Not architecture at scale — I've read the CV. A chartered forum that held decision rights and demonstrably retired standing committees. Name the place, the before, the after.

**JULIAN:** At Telstra International I was Global Head of Enterprise Solution Architecture — over two hundred million US of designs a year governed to one group standard across regions, with a design authority and a waiver process the regional teams couldn't route around. And I governed a three-hundred-million-plus managed-services transformation across many parallel streams to a single target without disruption. That's the governance muscle this role runs on.

**TY:** That's design assurance, Julian. I asked about committees. How many standing cross-region forums existed before your authority stood up, how many after, and what decision moved faster? Before and after.

**JULIAN:** ...Straight answer: I can't give you that number in those terms, and I won't construct one on the spot. What I've run are the load-bearing components — a standing authority, standards that held across regions, a waiver process with teeth, escalation that got used. What I haven't held is the charter to retire the committees around it; at Telstra those forums sat above my authority and stayed. So the honest statement: I've run every part of this machine, at larger scale than you need, but I haven't assembled the whole machine under one charter in one place. Which is exactly why the proposal I want to put to you doesn't ask you to bet on my track record at all.

**TY:** Go on — but log this, I'll come back to it: you're asking me to create a director-level role to run a machine you've never run end to end. That's a real gap, and if I put you in front of Tom Uva, he'll ask the same question less politely. What's the de-risk?

**JULIAN:** The first step isn't a hire — it's a two-week audit. The coordination cost is real but unquantified, and it's cheaply quantifiable: cadence, attendee lists, durations are all in the calendar. Finance ops pulls them in an afternoon. Convert the senior hours and reconciliation effort to money and you have a real number instead of my assertion. Then a pre-agreed gate decides the role, not faith: if measured coordination cost exceeds an agreed multiple of the fully-loaded cost of the role, it's chartered. If not, it dies and we both walk away clean. Cost of the audit: effectively zero. It replaces an argument with a number.

**TY:** Now you're speaking my language, so let me stress-test it, because it's either the smartest thing you've said or a stall dressed as rigour. Two pushbacks. One: you're not on payroll. If my finance ops can pull calendars, why do I need you in the loop — why isn't this my analyst's afternoon? Two: what's the multiple?

**JULIAN:** On the first: the data pull is your team's, and it should be — zero cost, no consultant invoice, that's the point. What I contribute is the method, because the definitions decide whether the number survives scrutiny. A retired committee has to mean a standing cross-region decision forum with recurring senior attendance, baselined by calendar audit not self-report — or the day this gets serious they all get renamed alignment syncs and the baseline evaporates. And the room hours are the small half; the bigger cost is decision latency — spend and projects stalled while a call bounces. Your team counts; I define what counts and how latency gets cased. On the multiple: I'd propose the structure and you set the number. If measured cost doesn't clear several times the loaded cost of the role, it shouldn't exist and I'd say so first.

**TY:** If I'm setting it, it's four or five times, not three — you just conceded execution risk, so the hurdle goes up. Fine. Now Tony. My actual problem, take it seriously, because I live with this man after you and I finish. The committees you want to retire are the rooms where Tony Chung's organisation exercises regional power. Tony runs technology for APAC and he's not going anywhere. The day this becomes real, what Tony hears is: a director outside my line, chartered by finance, is taking my rooms away. Walk me through how this isn't a war I started.

**JULIAN:** Three parts. First, structurally: the board pools authority, it doesn't confiscate it. Tony's directors sit on the board. Its decisions are their authority exercised together against an agreed standard — I chair the process, I don't outvote peers. Nobody loses a team, a budget, a line report. The board decides only the cross-region questions no single region can settle alone — the ones already leaving Tony's control and going to committee today. Second, sequencing: Tony hears before it reaches any sign-off desk, not after, and ideally from you, framed as what it is — his directors currently burn hundreds of senior hours a year in rooms that decide nothing, and this gives their decisions an answer in days instead of quarters. Third, resourcing is committed at charter — a named share of regional architects' time — so it never becomes a monthly negotiation about whether his people are too busy.

**TY:** Tony's counter is obvious, first meeting: "Fine, good idea, I'll run it. Give the board to one of my directors." Why is that wrong? Careful — I'll hear his version too.

**JULIAN:** Wrong structurally, not personally, and I'd say it the same to Tony. Any chair from inside one region inherits that region's allegiance — not a character flaw, an org-chart fact. The other regions and Milwaukee read every ruling through it, and the first waiver that goes Hong Kong's way confirms the suspicion whether it's fair or not. And the gap you described is cross-region including the US: you cannot coordinate peers from inside one peer's organisation. The role has to sit outside the regional lines to be neutral — that's the whole reason it doesn't exist yet, there's been nowhere neutral to put it.

**TY:** Which lands us at reporting lines, and here it gets awkward for me. If it doesn't sit under Tony, where? If it reports to me, the story writes itself: finance annexing technology. I've watched that movie. Finance guys who reach for the tech estate lose.

**JULIAN:** Agreed, and that's why finance alone can't hold it — same reason a finance-only charter fails. Chartered jointly by you and group technology leadership, dotted line into Tom Uva's organisation, sponsored by finance because finance owns the cost of the chaos. You're not annexing technology; you're the one who noticed what it costs. Tom's tier holds the line authority; you hold the business case. Neither can be accused of a land grab because neither did it alone.

**TY:** You need Tom on board before this is real. Noted — my homework, not yours, and I'm not committing today. Next: level and cost. Director level. The pushback you'll get from every desk including mine: I can hire a principal architect with the best AI tooling for half the money. Diagrams draw themselves now. Why not?

**JULIAN:** Because the diagram was never the hard part. The hard part is getting six regions to adopt one and stick to it — a decision-authority job, not a drawing job. You're right the artefacts are cheap; AI drafts candidate standards faster than any principal. But precisely because they're cheap, the scarce thing — the only thing you'd pay for — is the standing to make a call stick across your directors. A principal is an individual contributor; no mandate to hold a decision against a director, by definition. The cheap version isn't cheaper — it's a full salary for a role that structurally can't deliver, plus another year of coordination tax. TTI APAC has multiple technology directors; coordinating across them takes peer-level authority. Director is the floor because of your org chart, not my ambitions.

**TY:** Best version of that answer I've heard, and I've heard it a few times. Last structural one. Timing. Frank leaves within weeks. His successor won't be functional until well into the fall. Every instinct here says park anything new until the new CFO lands. Zero-mess move. Convince me not to.

**JULIAN:** The design already assumes it — the charter deliberately doesn't rest on Frank. But more to the point: a transition quarter is exactly what the audit is for. It's costless, commits you to nothing, and produces the one thing your incoming CFO will demand anyway before signing any structure — a measured number. Park everything and you donate another quarter of coordination tax and hand your new CFO a blank page. Run the audit and you hand them a dollarised problem with a pre-agreed gate on the fix, on day one. Which finance leader do you want to be in that first meeting? And let me turn one back: what's the cross-region technology decision that's been bouncing longest on your desk right now?

**TY:** ...The EBS Hong Kong question. What replaces it, on whose platform, on whose budget. Circling committees over a year, and every quarter it doesn't land the migration window prices up. Don't quote me outside this room.

**JULIAN:** Then that's your latency exhibit, worth more than my whole model. The audit dollarises the room hours; that one story evidences the real cost — a year of a stalled platform decision. And if the role gets chartered, that's the first decision I'd put through the board as the proof: one live cross-region question, settled through the mechanism inside ninety days, instead of another four quarters of committee.

**TY:** Careful — that item's radioactive precisely because it's Tony's estate, so it may be the proof case or the grenade, and picking which is a judgment made jointly. Okay. Stephan says you're committed to Hong Kong — right? I don't build a role around someone with one foot on a plane.

**JULIAN:** I'm staying in Hong Kong. If this gets built, I'm building it here, long term — a governance structure only pays off if the person who stands it up is there to hold it in year two and three. That's the job I want.

**TY:** Good. Where I am. The diagnosis I already believed — it's mine. The machinery is better thought through than I expected; the SLA and ratification design is the first version of "governance" anyone's brought me that isn't just a new committee. The audit's the right first step and cheap enough that I can own it without asking permission. Three things before anything moves. One: send me the audit on one page — method, definitions, gate structure with the multiple blank for me to set, and the loaded cost of the role at HK market so the gate has a denominator. Two: your answer to "where have you done this," in writing, tightened — when I socialise this with Tom Uva he will ask it, and "honest gap plus every component" needs to survive a second telling by someone who isn't you. Three: nothing reaches Tony until the number exists and I decide how he hears it. That sequencing is mine. Fair?

**JULIAN:** Fair on all three. You'll have the page and the written answer this week. One ask back: when you talk to Tom, put the design requirement in front of him rather than the candidate — joint charter, decision SLA, single ratifier — and let him shoot at the mechanism. If it survives him, the role conversation is easy. If it doesn't, better we know before anyone's hired.

**TY:** Right order, and conveniently the order that protects you. I noticed. Send me the page, Julian.

## 2. Failure points (weighted by damage)

1. **Prior-proof answered with scale instead of mechanism (highest).** Ty is immune to big numbers; he asked before/after on committees. Scale reads as evasion, converts "receptive" to "cautious," and gets retold to Uva as "couldn't answer the track-record question." **Move:** confession welded to the audit de-risk in the same breath — *"I haven't run this exact machine end to end under one charter, and I won't pretend otherwise. What I've run are its load-bearing parts at larger scale than you need... which is exactly why my ask isn't 'trust my track record' — it's a two-week audit with a threshold that kills the role if the number isn't there."* Pre-build one concrete Telstra before/after; the bridge survives Ty once, never survives Uva.
2. **Charter-sponsor as a blank instead of co-design.** Naming a sponsor wrongly (can't read the org from outside) or visibly having no answer both expose scaffolding-around-a-blank. **Move:** present as design requirement, hand Ty the pen. Trap-inside-trap: have the **CEO-chip fallback** ready (day-to-day ratifier at Uva tier; CEO terminus a rarely-used backstop formalised after a track record) or the qualification-gate line boomerangs into "so the role can't exist."
3. **School deadline / UK leaking to Ty** re-arms the flight-risk read Stephan cleared. **Move:** to Ty it's pure fit/value forever; timing pressure rides the Stephan channel only. If probed: the commitment answer ("I'm staying... year two and three").
4. **Answering "why not give it to Tony?" about Tony instead of structure.** Any evaluation of Tony imports a feud into Ty's ears. **Move:** "structurally, not personally — any chair inside one region inherits its allegiance," then hand Ty the Tony *plan* (hears first, from Ty, framed as his directors benefiting), not a Tony grievance.
5. **AI line detached from the authority weld** hands Ty "a principal plus Copilot in Uva's org." **Move:** artefacts cheap → the only thing paid for is standing to make a call stick → cheap version can't deliver. Cost logic, not title logic.
6. **Audit read as a stall, OR captured away from Julian** ("great, my finance ops runs it, thanks" — designs Julian out of his own on-ramp; the more likely and dangerous variant). **Move:** split data (their team) from method (Julian's — the definitions that make the number survive), and carry a real HK loaded-cost denominator so the gate isn't empty.
7. **Frank-transition park accepted, or room pushed for a verdict.** **Move:** reframe the transition as the audit's argument (produces what the incoming CFO demands anyway); close on the smallest Ty-executable action — approve the one-page audit scope — never the role.

## 3. Conversion plan — Ty from "described a gap" to "active sponsor"

**Core mechanic: transfer authorship of the *solution shape*** (he already owns the diagnosis). Every load-bearing element leaves with Ty's fingerprints: Ty sets the gate multiple, Ty picks the escalation construct with Uva, Ty owns the Tony sequencing, Ty supplies the latency exhibit (the EBS HK decision only he can name). A man who's made four small decisions about a structure is sponsoring it whether he's said the word or not — the Kari attribution mechanic, at Ty's altitude: hand over the control points, don't hide the machinery.

**Commitment ladder (each rung small, Ty-executable, cover-preserving):** (1) in-room: Ty agrees to receive the one-page audit scope and sets/agrees-to-set the multiple; (2) within the week: Julian delivers the page + the written prior-proof answer, both Uva-grade; (3) Ty socialises the *mechanism* not the candidate with Uva (a no-lose move for Ty); (4) the audit runs under finance ops with Julian's method — Ty's initiative on record; (5) Ty sequences Tony after the number exists, framed as a gift; (6) charter drafted jointly Ty+Uva, the gate number decides the role.

**What Ty personally needs:** a number he can carry upward (Deputy-CFO-becomes-CFO story, live because Frank's leaving); cover at every step (the gate is his insurance); a co-sponsor in Uva (never "finance annexing technology"); a Tony story that looks like statesmanship not conquest; confidence Julian won't embarrass him in front of Uva (why the prior-proof answer must be fixed even though Ty accepted the honest version — his real risk is reputational, secondhand).

**What Julian must NOT do:** ask for the role (ask = the next smallest step); mention UK/school/deadline (Stephan channel only); criticise Tony / quote history / ask Ty to overrule him (give a Tony *plan* not a *problem*); anchor a salary (loaded cost enters only as the gate denominator); send the CFO brief before this meeting's read / the two numbers / Ty controlling Tony sequencing; claim SAP/Oracle depth or drop the AI line without the weld; repeat the qualification-gate line more than once (said twice it becomes a list of reasons the role can't exist); push Ty to commit in the room (he converts by making small decisions privately then defending them).
