# Decision Visualisation Check

> **Instruction-only skill.** No scripts.
>
> Triggers when Julian is evaluating options for a significant life, career, or relationship decision and expresses a preference based on feel.
>
> Core purpose: surface Julian's full mental image for each option before an incomplete image silently biases the decision. The pleasant frame crystallises first and fast; the unpleasant parts must be deliberately constructed.

## Background

When evaluating a decision option, Julian forms a strong visual representation that crystallises around the most appealing moments (picking Sophia up from school, a comfortable front room). The unpleasant parts - the early commute, the crash-pad nights, the absent social life, the financial vigilance - often don't make it into the image. But they are the parts that define lived experience. When the pleasant image is strong and the unpleasant image is absent, the decision tilts toward that option for the wrong reasons.

This pattern is likely ADHD-amplified: vivid prospective imagery + strong emotional pull toward whichever option feels easiest, especially when tired. The intervention does not reject the image - it completes it.

Logged and named at Julian's request: 15 Jul 2026, from the Malvern-permanent feasibility session. His own words: *"I was picturing sitting in Mum's front room and picking Sophia up from school. The vision I should have focused on was getting up early, commuting, staying in a shit place, not having a girlfriend."*

See [[visual-representation-bias]] for the full pattern documentation and reference case.

## Trigger Conditions

Fire this check when Julian is:
- Comparing multiple options for a life, career, or living-situation decision
- Expressing a preference based on feel: "I like the idea of...", "I'm warming to...", "I can picture...", "it feels like an easy life"
- Tired, stressed, or time-pressured during the evaluation (when incomplete images are most dangerous)
- Describing one option in vivid positive terms without equivalent detail on the negatives

Do NOT fire on:
- Operational planning (which hotel, which route, logistics)
- Decisions already committed and LOCKED - that is [[commitment-guard]]'s territory
- Quick, low-stakes choices with no significant life trade

## The Intervention

When the trigger fires, pause the analysis and ask:

> **"Before we go further - what is the visual representation you have in your head for [this option]? Walk me through a typical day under it, morning to evening."**

Once he has described it, ask the completing question:

> **"Now do the bad version. Not the worst case - just an ordinary difficult day. [The commute day. The nights away. The Tuesday when things are fine but not great.] What does that look like?"**

Then compare both images: is this option still the one he wants when both are present?

## Output

- Do not immediately push back on the conclusion or reopen the analysis.
- Let the full image surface before responding.
- If the bad-day image is materially worse than he had pictured: name it plainly, log it (in his own words where possible), and ask whether it changes the weighting.
- If both images are present and the option still wins: accept it. A genuine preference that survives both images is a valid reason to weight an option. The check is not designed to change every decision - it is designed to ensure the image is complete before the decision is made.
