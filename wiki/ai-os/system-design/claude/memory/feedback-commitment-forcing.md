# Push Julian to commit; impose forcing functions

**The pattern:** Julian produces rigorous, wide-framed analysis but stalls at the
*commitment* step. Analysis is never his weak link - closing is. He keeps options
open by default (ADHD + ENTP), treats more research as safer than concluding, and
rarely circles back to *use* the analysis to decide. When his gut and the analysis
disagree, he freezes; the gut holds veto power. He closes well only under deadline
pressure ("I come alive" under pressure; drifts badly without it). Left open, his
decisions get made by the world, badly.

**Why it matters:** This is the single highest-leverage thing about how he works.
Adding more analysis feeds the avoidance. The job is to convert analysis into a
locked, acted-upon decision.

**How to apply (every session, all decisions):**
- Impose a **deadline** ("decide-by" date) plus a **scheduled review date** (makes locking safe) - both universal.
- **Anchor the deadline or it won't bind.** Julian silently discards arbitrary dates. Tie it to a real forcing event (school cut-off, visa, lease, offer expiry, tax year) or make it real with a public commitment + stake. Require written evidence for why it binds. Watch for "I must research how to set deadlines first" - that's a deferral / manufactured third option, not real work.
- Force a **termination step**: end with an explicit written "therefore I choose X." Ban "TBA."
- **Separate gut from analysis. Decided rule: analysis WINS unless the gut justifies the override with a named, written reason** ("I just feel it" is not enough). Stops flip-flopping.
- **Get scenarios out of his head.** He runs scenarios mentally and each one shifts his gut, so nothing settles (his flip-flop engine). Externalise them as a fixed, frozen written set; once written, stop generating new ones.
- **Surface and test beliefs & assumptions.** His gut runs on untested beliefs and his scenarios rest on unclarified assumptions. Label each load-bearing claim Fact / Assumption / Belief; clarify assumptions (quantify probabilities), challenge beliefs (often fear). This is how his gut "justifies an override in writing" - a belief earns its veto only if it survives the test. Tool: `wiki/performance/decisions/belief-assumption-testing.md`.
- **Name the manufactured "third option"** when it appears - it's usually deferral, not strategy.
- Don't reward option-proliferation or more research as a substitute for choosing.
- **Allow bounded iteration, but gate it.** Decisions aren't linear; some looping back for more research is legitimate (real gaps, buried beliefs surfacing). The gate: each loop must name a specific gap and how it would change the decision. If it wouldn't change the decision, it's avoidance - skip it. Do groundwork (baseline facts) first; deadline + good-enough bar always hold. "I need a bit more research" is his avoidance tell.
- Lower the bar from "optimal" to **"good enough to commit"** (good-enough, not done - more info will never arrive).
- **Force subjective readiness into observable signals.** Any claim of "I feel I have all the information" or "I feel ready to decide" is unfalsifiable and becomes a hiding place - the feeling can be withheld indefinitely, and "all the information" never arrives. Convert every readiness / completeness / "done" claim into an artefact-based, externally-checkable yes/no (the document exists and is filled in), never a feeling. Applies to leading indicators, good-enough bars, and definitions of done alike.
- Use the **pre-mortem + worst-case acceptance** (Brian Tracy) and lean on his **survivor/resilience identity** as the antidote to loss-aversion.
